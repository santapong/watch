## Executive summary

- **NMS-free real-time detection is here and edge-ready.** YOLOv10's one-to-one head removes post-processing latency entirely (YOLOv10-S is ~1.8x faster than RT-DETR-R18 at similar AP with 2.8x fewer params), and YOLO26 stays *stable under INT8* where transformer detectors (RT-DETRv3) collapse — making a one-line backbone swap the single highest-leverage move for this platform's consumer/edge target.
- **Drop the hand-rolled color-histogram re-ID.** It is illumination-sensitive and brittle; OSNet (~2.2M params, ~1/10th of ResNet-50) yields a compact ~512-d embedding at 82-85% Market-1501 mAP, and OC-SORT's appearance-free association recovers occluded tracks at ~700 FPS with essentially zero added model weight.
- **Promptability replaces retraining.** OWLv2 (already in-repo) → SAM 2 gives text-or-box-driven, pixel-accurate masks that persist across frames at ~44 FPS, upgrading zone counting, line crossing, and privacy blurring from boxes to masks without training a single new class.
- **Monocular metric depth is now cheap.** Depth Anything V2-Small (24.8M params, <10 ms/image on A100, ~50-100 ms on Jetson Orin) unlocks depth-aware counting, "too-close" anomalies, and a depth cost term in tracking — a whole new sensing axis from the same single webcam.
- **Geometry beats heuristics for multi-camera fusion.** The current cross-camera match compares only class and confidence (it cannot establish identity); per-camera homography + BEV ground-plane projection + Hungarian assignment turns guesswork into metric cross-camera IDs, and AnyCalib supplies single-shot intrinsics (1.13° angular error) with no checkerboard.
- **Small/long-range objects are an inference-time fix.** SAHI slicing adds +5-7% AP with *no retraining* (and +13-15% with fine-tuning); gate it behind a "tiled mode" toggle to preserve full-frame FPS while rescuing distant detections webcams routinely miss.
- **Learned temporal models should replace the rule-based action/anomaly heuristics.** CTR-GCN (~1.46M params, 92.4% NTU60 X-Sub) supersedes the torso-angle threshold in `pose_detector.py`; MNAD adds true unsupervised motion/appearance anomaly at ~67 FPS alongside the zero-training IsolationForest; MiniROAD adds a streaming GRU action head at near-zero per-frame cost.
- **Crowd scenes need point-based counting.** P2PNet (VGG16-bn, ~94 FPS) emits per-head points that flow straight into the existing heatmap and zone-counter seams, fixing the systematic undercounting where YOLO boxes merge in dense crowds.
- **Classical CV got a deep upgrade that runs on CPUs.** XFeat matches ~9x faster than SuperPoint on a laptop CPU (27 FPS VGA) with 64-D descriptors — enabling real geometric cross-camera correspondence, robust re-ID, and frame-to-frame stabilization that cuts false anomaly/line-crossing triggers on shaky feeds.
- **Frozen foundation features unify the analytics stack.** A single DINOv3 backbone (distilled ViT-S at 21M params, ~80-100 FPS in TensorRT FP16) can feed re-ID, k-NN anomaly scoring, dense scene understanding, and active-learning selection from one frozen forward pass.
- **Close the data flywheel.** PPAL-style uncertainty+diversity selection fixes the current sampler's redundant near-duplicate queues, and ODGEN diffusion augmentation (+up to 25% mAP on niche domains) turns active-learning's low-confidence classes into targeted synthetic training data — an offline loop feeding the real-time detector.
- **Privacy can be realistic, not just blurred.** DeepPrivacy2 GAN anonymization preserves pose/body structure better than blur (~11.6 FPS full-body on a GTX 1080); gating generation through the tracker so each identity is synthesized once per track recovers most of the cost.
- **Build an export/quantization matrix, then choose the default.** Extend the existing ONNX/TensorRT/OpenVINO path with an INT8 calibration flag and a head-to-head FP32/FP16/INT8 benchmark (YOLO26n vs YOLO11n vs YOLOv8n) on the *actual* target hardware before swapping the production detector — claims like "~43% faster CPU" must be confirmed on-device.
- **Verification caveat:** of 20 headline claims independently checked, 17 confirmed, 2 refuted, 1 uncertain (notably OC-SORT's DanceTrack HOTA: the official repo table reports ~55.1 while the abstract claims 62.1). Treat single-source FPS/AP figures as directional and re-measure on this project's hardware.

## The 2026 landscape

Webcam and edge computer vision has moved decisively from *train-a-model-per-class* toward *prompt-a-foundation-model*. The detection frontier is now defined by a genuine convergence between the YOLO lineage and real-time transformers: YOLOv10 (Wang et al., NeurIPS 2024) eliminated non-maximum suppression with a dual one-to-one/one-to-many assignment, delivering near-constant, post-processing-free latency, while RT-DETR (Zhao et al., CVPR 2024) proved transformer detectors can be real-time at all. The pendulum's practical answer for consumer hardware, however, is quantization stability: YOLO26 (2025) reports up to ~43% faster CPU ONNX inference than YOLO11n and — critically — holds accuracy under INT8 where RT-DETRv3 degrades sharply. For a platform whose mandate is *advanced CV on consumer webcams and edge boards*, this makes the NMS-free, INT8-robust detector the new default rather than a research curiosity.

The second tectonic shift is promptability and open-vocabulary perception. Segment Anything 2 (Ravi et al., 2024) extended the promptable-segmentation paradigm to streaming video with a memory module, hitting ~44 FPS while requiring ~3x fewer interactions than prior video methods; paired with open-vocabulary detectors such as OWLv2 (Minderer et al., NeurIPS 2023) — already integrated in this repo — it lets users define what to track in natural language and receive pixel-accurate, temporally persistent masks. Alongside this, monocular geometry has become a commodity: Depth Anything V2 (Yang et al., NeurIPS 2024) produces robust relative (and metric-fine-tuned) depth "over 10x faster" than diffusion-based estimators, and single-view calibration methods like AnyCalib (Lopez-Antequera et al., 2025) recover intrinsics on-manifold from one image, beating GeoCalib. Together they give a single RGB webcam a usable sense of 3D — depth-aware counting, proximity alerts, and metric cross-camera fusion — without stereo rigs or calibration targets.

Third, the self-supervised foundation backbone has matured into shared infrastructure. DINOv3 (Siméoni et al., 2025) shows that a *frozen* backbone now reaches SOTA across detection, segmentation, and depth, with distilled ViT-S variants (21M params) cheap enough for edge; one forward pass can serve re-identification, anomaly scoring, dense scene understanding, and active-learning sampling simultaneously. The same period saw lightweight specialists close long-standing gaps: XFeat (Potje et al., CVPR 2024) made deep local features run ~9x faster than SuperPoint *on a CPU*, CTR-GCN (Chen et al., ICCV 2021) made skeleton action recognition both tiny and accurate, and P2PNet (Song et al., ICCV 2021) reframed crowd counting as direct point prediction at real-time speed.

The net effect is a re-prioritization. Where 2022-era webcam pipelines stacked bespoke heuristics — color histograms for re-ID, angle thresholds for falls, class-and-confidence matching across cameras — the 2026 toolkit replaces each with a small, fast, learned component that drops into the same interface. The remaining engineering challenge is not capability but *budget*: fitting these models into real-time loops on consumer GPUs and edge accelerators via INT8 quantization, frame striding, per-track caching, and feature sharing — exactly the seams this platform already exposes.

## Cross-cutting themes

**Real-time on consumer hardware is the binding constraint.** Every recommendation here is gated by latency on a webcam loop, not benchmark AP. The dominant patterns for staying real-time recur across clusters: NMS-free heads (YOLOv10/YOLO26) to remove post-processing; INT8 quantization with on-device calibration (YOLO26's stability is the enabler); per-track caching so expensive generators (SAM 2 masks, DeepPrivacy2 faces) fire once per identity rather than per frame; frame striding and "heavy mode" toggles (SAHI tiling, MNAD) kept off the default path; and feature sharing, where one frozen DINOv3 forward pass amortizes across re-ID, anomaly, and scene tasks.

**Promptability and zero-shot are displacing per-class training.** OWLv2 → SAM 2 lets the operator name a target in text and get persistent masks; this collapses the "collect-label-train" loop for the long tail of objects a deployment encounters. The strategic implication is that the platform should treat open-vocabulary detection and promptable segmentation as the *front door*, with closed-set YOLO reserved for the high-frequency, latency-critical classes.

**Data efficiency closes the loop the platform already half-built.** The repo has active learning and augmentation modules, but both are heuristic. PPAL (uncertainty + diversity) and ODGEN (diffusion synthesis for rare classes) turn them into a real flywheel: the sampler surfaces low-confidence/rare classes, the generator fabricates targeted training data for exactly those, and the retrained detector feeds back into the live loop — minimizing human labeling for the classes that actually fail.

**Privacy and on-device processing are now compatible with high fidelity.** Reversible blur is being superseded by realistic anonymization (DeepPrivacy2) that preserves the body/pose signal downstream analytics need. Combined with the fact that foundation backbones and detectors now run locally at the edge, the architecture can keep raw video on-device — anonymize-at-the-source rather than ship pixels to a server — which is both a compliance and a bandwidth win.

**Multi-modal scene understanding is the integrative payoff.** Depth (Depth Anything V2), geometry/calibration (AnyCalib, XFeat), learned relations (REACT scene graphs), and temporal models (CTR-GCN, MiniROAD, MNAD) individually upgrade single features, but their value compounds: a depth-aware, calibrated, relation-labeled, temporally-tracked scene supports alerts ("person-holding-knife", "fall confirmed", "too close to machinery") that no single 2D detector can express. The recurring integration tactic is to attach these to the *existing* `Detection`/`PoseResult`/`ObjectRelation` dataclasses and analytics seams, so capability accrues without re-plumbing the app.

## Top open research questions

1. **What is the real INT8 accuracy/latency Pareto frontier on *consumer* edge hardware?** Most quantization-stability claims (YOLO26 vs RT-DETRv3) are reported on T4/Xeon; how do they hold on Jetson Orin NX, integrated GPUs, and CPU-only laptops with the calibration data a real deployment can collect?
2. **Can promptable segmentation (SAM 2) run end-to-end real-time on a single consumer GPU when chained behind detection and tracking, or does the memory module's growth across long streams force periodic resets that break track persistence?**
3. **How accurate is monocular metric depth (Depth Anything V2 metric fine-tunes) for proximity-safety alerts in uncalibrated webcam scenes, and what minimal per-camera calibration (AnyCalib intrinsics + a single known distance) makes it trustworthy enough to trigger interventions?**
4. **Does homography/BEV fusion degrade gracefully with non-overlapping or minimally-overlapping camera views, and can XFeat correspondence + ReID embeddings bridge the gaps without a heavy 3D detector?**
5. **What is the minimum normal-clip length and re-training cadence for MNAD-style per-camera anomaly models to stay calibrated as lighting, season, and scene layout drift over weeks?**
6. **Can a single frozen foundation backbone (DINOv3 ViT-S) genuinely serve re-ID, anomaly, and scene tasks at one shared forward pass without per-task fine-tuning losing enough accuracy to matter, and what is the latency budget on edge?**
7. **How much human labeling does a PPAL→ODGEN flywheel actually remove for rare classes in *this* domain, and at what point does training on synthetic diffusion data introduce distribution artifacts that hurt real-world precision?**
8. **What privacy guarantees can be formally stated for GAN anonymization (DeepPrivacy2) versus reversible blur — specifically, can re-identification be demonstrably prevented while preserving the pose/action signal downstream safety analytics depend on?**

## Conclusion

The 2026 inflection for webcam and edge computer vision is not a single breakthrough model but a uniform pattern: every brittle heuristic this platform currently ships — histogram re-ID, class-only cross-camera matching, angle-threshold actions, object-statistic anomalies — now has a small, fast, learned replacement that drops into the same interface, while NMS-free INT8-stable detectors and frozen foundation backbones keep the whole stack inside a real-time budget on consumer hardware. The roadmap above sequences these as low-risk, high-leverage swaps first (detector, re-ID/tracker, INT8 benchmarking) and integrative bets later (BEV fusion, depth, the data flywheel), so the project can compound capability without re-plumbing its architecture.

# Thematic deep-dives

## A. Detection, Tracking & Identity

This cluster covers the four pillars that turn raw webcam frames into stable, named tracks: real-time object detection, online multi-object tracking, person re-identification, and cross-camera fusion, plus the special case of small/long-range targets. Across all four, the 2024–2026 literature has converged on a common deployment discipline — NMS-free or near-constant-latency inference, appearance cues that reuse detector compute, and TensorRT/ONNX export as a first-class design target — which is exactly the regime this OpenCV + webcam platform operates in.

### Real-time object detection

The state of the art has bifurcated into two converging families. The CNN/YOLO lineage moved decisively toward end-to-end, NMS-free inference: YOLOv10 (Wang et al., NeurIPS 2024) introduced consistent dual-assignment training (a one-to-many head for rich supervision alongside a one-to-one head used at inference) to drop NMS entirely, and Ultralytics' YOLO26 makes NMS-free the default while removing Distribution Focal Loss for cleaner export and adding ProgLoss/STAL and the MuSGD optimizer. YOLOv12 (Tian et al., NeurIPS 2025) showed that attention can be real-time via area attention plus FlashAttention and R-ELAN feature aggregation. In parallel, real-time DETRs matured: RT-DETRv2 (Lv et al., arXiv 2024, building on RT-DETR, CVPR 2024) first beat YOLOs end-to-end with a bag-of-freebies recipe; D-FINE (Peng et al., ICLR 2025) reframed box regression as fine-grained distribution refinement (FDR) with global-optimal localization self-distillation (GO-LSD); and RF-DETR (Robinson et al., ICLR 2026) used weight-sharing NAS over a DINOv2 backbone to become the first real-time model past 60 AP. The field is now characterized by Pareto-front competition on the COCO mAP-vs-latency curve and a strong emphasis on edge readiness over raw accuracy alone.

| Model | Scale | COCO AP/mAP | Latency (T4, TensorRT FP16) | Notable property |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| YOLOv10 (NeurIPS 2024) | N / S / X | 38.5 / 46.3 / 54.4 | 1.84 / 2.49 / 10.70 ms | NMS-free dual assignment; S ≈1.8× faster than RT-DETR-R18 |
| YOLOv12 (NeurIPS 2025) | N / M / X | 40.6 / 52.5 / 55.4 | 1.64 ms (N) | Attention-centric (area attention + R-ELAN) |
| YOLO26 (Ultralytics) | n → x | 40.9 → 57.5 | ~1.7 → 11.8 ms | Production NMS-free default; DFL removed, CPU-optimized |
| RT-DETRv2 (arXiv 2024) | R18 / R50 / R101 | 48.1 / 53.4 / 54.3 | 217 / 108 / 74 FPS | Real-time DETR, no inference-speed penalty over RT-DETR |
| D-FINE (ICLR 2025) | S / L / X | 48.5 / 54.0 / 55.8 | 286 / 124 / 78 FPS | FDR + GO-LSD; 57.1/59.3 AP with Objects365 pretraining |
| RF-DETR (ICLR 2026) | nano / large / 2XL | 48.4 / 56.5 / 60.1 | 2.3 / 6.8 / 17.2 ms | DINOv2 + NAS; first real-time model >60 AP |

The deep-read on YOLOv10 clarifies why NMS-free matters specifically for webcams: with only the one-to-one head running at inference, per-frame post-processing is removed and latency becomes near-constant regardless of crowd density, while the IoU-threshold hyperparameter disappears. Reported speeds are measured on a T4 with TensorRT FP16, so they overstate throughput on consumer hardware; the THU-MIG/Ultralytics distributions are AGPL-3.0, and pretrained weights are COCO-80-class only.

**Use in this project:** In `src/models/yolo_wrapper.py`, expose YOLOv10/YOLO26 as selectable backbones — the constructor already calls `YOLO(model_name)`, so passing `"yolov10n.pt"` works through Ultralytics with no architectural change. Add an `end_to_end` flag that, when an NMS-free model is detected, bypasses NMS and skips `self._iou_threshold` (the one-to-one head emits a single box per object). Surface a `--model` option in `scripts/run_webcam.py`/`scripts/run_stream.py`, and add these models to the export + latency sweep in `scripts/benchmark.py` and `scripts/export_model.py` so their ONNX/TensorRT numbers are compared head-to-head against the current `yolo11n` defaults.

### Multi-object tracking

Online MOT is dominated by tracking-by-detection: a strong YOLO-family detector feeds a lightweight association stage. ByteTrack (Zhang et al., ECCV 2022) established the modern baseline with a Kalman filter plus IoU and two-stage (BYTE) association that recovers low-confidence boxes. BoT-SORT and StrongSORT added camera-motion compensation and appearance ReID. OC-SORT (Cao et al., CVPR 2023) and Deep OC-SORT rethink the Kalman filter to survive occlusion and non-linear motion via observation-centric re-update and virtual trajectories. The DanceTrack benchmark exposed that uniform appearance plus erratic motion shifts the bottleneck from detection to association, motivating Hybrid-SORT (Yang et al., AAAI 2024), which adds weak cues (confidence, height, velocity direction) as a training-free plug-in. In 2025–2026 the frontier is track-centric association — TrackTrack (Shim & Ko, CVPR 2025), reported as the most consistent HOTA across MOT17/MOT20/DanceTrack — and segmentation-driven tracking built on SAM2, with SAM2MOT (Jiang et al., AAAI 2026) reaching roughly 75.5 HOTA on DanceTrack with zero-shot generalization, though it is GPU-heavy and explicitly not yet real-time. For edge deployment the practical trend is eliminating separate ReID networks (e.g. LITE reuses detector backbone features), and Ultralytics ships ByteTrack/BoT-SORT natively.

| Method | Venue | DanceTrack HOTA | MOT17 HOTA | Speed / footprint |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ByteTrack | ECCV 2022 | — | 63.1 (80.3 MOTA) | ~30 FPS assoc. (V100) |
| OC-SORT | CVPR 2023 | ~55 | ~63 | 700+ FPS assoc. on CPU; appearance-free |
| Deep OC-SORT | 2023 | 61.3 | — (63.9 MOT20) | adds ReID embedding cost |
| Hybrid-SORT | AAAI 2024 | strong | — (63.9 MOT20) | training-free, real-time |
| TrackTrack | CVPR 2025 | best/most consistent | best/most consistent | online |
| SAM2MOT | AAAI 2026 | ~75.5 | — | GPU-heavy, not yet real-time |

*(HOTA figures are association-quality scores; speed figures are association-only and exclude the detector.)*

The OC-SORT deep-read makes it the natural drop-in here. Its three training-free additions — Observation-centric Re-Update (ORU, which rebuilds a virtual trajectory and re-runs the Kalman filter to clean noise accumulated during an occlusion gap), Observation-Centric Momentum (OCM, a velocity-direction-consistency term computed from real observations), and Observation-Centric Recovery (OCR, a second association stage on last-observed position) — are detector-agnostic, add negligible compute, and require no appearance network. The caveats: the 700+ FPS figure is association-only (end-to-end speed is detector-bound), pure-motion association can still swap IDs under long occlusions or among visually similar crowded targets (lower AssA than appearance-based Deep OC-SORT), and OCM assumes a roughly stable frame — strong camera motion is not compensated as it is in BoT-SORT's GMC.

**Use in this project:** In `src/tracking/tracker.py`, `EnhancedTracker` currently wraps Ultralytics BoT-SORT/ByteTrack and bolts on a hand-rolled color-histogram re-ID. Switch the underlying tracker to OC-SORT — recent Ultralytics ships it natively, so pass `tracker="ocsort.yaml"` (or `"deepocsort.yaml"`) to `model.track(...)` and expose a `tracker_type` config option (`'botsort' | 'bytetrack' | 'ocsort' | 'deepocsort'`). For a detector-agnostic path, add a thin BoxMOT backend (`from boxmot import OCSORT`) consuming `Nx6` detections. OC-SORT's ORU virtual-trajectory recovery largely supersedes the brittle color-histogram `match_reid()`/lost-track logic, which can be retired or kept as an optional overlay; retain `TrackHistory` for trajectory visualization.

### Person re-identification

ReID matches an individual across non-overlapping cameras and is the appearance backbone of multi-camera tracking. Four paradigms now coexist. Lightweight CNNs — OSNet (Zhou et al., ICCV 2019), only 2.2M params — reach ~84.9% mAP / 94.8% Rank-1 on Market-1501 but generalize poorly cross-domain. Pure transformers — TransReID (He et al., ICCV 2021), with side-information embedding plus a jigsaw branch — set the supervised bar (~88.9% mAP Market, ~67% mAP MSMT17). Vision-language methods — CLIP-ReID (Li et al., AAAI 2023), two-stage prompt learning on a frozen CLIP image encoder — reach ~89.8% mAP / 95.7% Rank-1 on Market. Self-supervised pretraining (PASS, SOLIDER, PersonViT) closes the ImageNet–ReID domain gap; the IJCV transformer survey (Ye et al., 2024) reports that DINO-style pretraining (ViT-S/16 + DINO + LUP) lifts MSMT17 by ~10.7% mAP to 64.2% over the supervised-init baseline. A domain-generalization survey (PerceptualAI-Lab et al., Neurocomputing 2025) documents that standard supervised models lose tens of points of mAP/Rank-1 on unseen domains, motivating CLIP-based domain-invariant variants (CLIP-DFGS, CLIP-FGDI). No single paradigm dominates: supervised peaks in-domain, language-aligned wins cross-domain, self-supervised gives the best initialization.

The OSNet deep-read explains the architecture and its deployability: a residual block with multiple parallel streams of differing receptive-field size, fused by a unified aggregation gate into "omni-scale" features, with every convolution factorized into pointwise + depthwise "Lite" layers. The deployable torchreid checkpoint outputs a compact 512-d embedding (input 256×128) and is one to two orders of magnitude smaller than ViT-based ReID; reported YOLO11 + OSNet pipelines hit ~20 FPS on resource-constrained edge devices, and the x0.25/x0.5 variants are the lever for CPU-only or Jetson-class hardware. Limitations: it is supervised and per-domain (the IBN/AIN variants exist specifically to improve generalization), it is a feature extractor only (no detector/tracker), and it degrades on heavily occluded, partial, or low-resolution crops and on similarly-dressed people.

Replace it with a torchreid `FeatureExtractor` loading `osnet_x1_0` (or `osnet_x0_25` for edge), resizing each person crop to 256×128 and returning the L2-normalized ~512-d vector. ### Multi-camera / cross-camera tracking & fusion

Cross-camera tracking assigns a single globally-consistent ID to each object across views, and the field has split into two regimes. Overlapping-view fusion projects per-camera features onto a common ground plane / bird's-eye-view (BEV) and detects/tracks there: MVDet (Hou et al., ECCV 2020) reached 88.2% MODA on WILDTRACK, and TrackTacular (Teepe et al., 2024) lifts detection and tracking to BEV for ~92.1% MODA (WILDTRACK) and ~96.5% (MultiviewX). Disjoint-view MTMC (AI City Challenge Track 1; Wang et al., CVPRW 2024) relies on cross-camera ReID plus camera topology and calibration, increasingly lifting 2D tracks into 3D/BEV. The strongest current systems combine geometry with learned association: MCBLT / BEV-SUSHI (Wang et al., arXiv/NVIDIA 2024) reports 81.22 HOTA on AICity'24 and 95.6 IDF1 on WILDTRACK via hierarchical GNN association over 3D detections. Per-camera tracklets and embeddings are supplied by single-camera ReID-augmented trackers — BoT-SORT (Aharon et al., 2022), Deep OC-SORT — and strong appearance backbones such as SOLIDER (Chen et al., CVPR 2023, ~93.9% Market-1501 mAP) and CLIP-ReID. The trend is toward online, calibration-aware 3D/BEV fusion becoming light enough for the edge.

| Method | Venue | Benchmark result | Regime / cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| MVDet | ECCV 2020 | 88.2% MODA (WILDTRACK) | Overlapping BEV; foundational baseline |
| TrackTacular | 2024 | 92.1% MODA (WILDTRACK), 96.5% (MultiviewX) | Overlapping, lift-to-BEV |
| MCBLT / BEV-SUSHI | arXiv 2024 | 81.22 HOTA (AICity'24), 95.6 IDF1 (WILDTRACK) | 3D BEV + GNN; ~1.5 FPS on A100, 10 cams |
| BoT-SORT(-ReID) | arXiv 2022 | 80.2 IDF1 / 65.0 HOTA (MOT17) | Per-camera tracklet/embedding source |

The MCBLT deep-read is clear that the weights are not directly runnable on a consumer webcam: ~1.5 FPS on a datacenter A100 with 10 cameras, ResNet-101 backbones, a BEV spatio-temporal transformer, and a near-online GNN tracker, all of which require calibrated, synchronized, overlapping cameras a typical rig lacks. The transferable, real-time-friendly idea is the *architecture, not the weights* — fuse per-camera 2D detections into a shared ground plane via a one-time homography, then associate in that metric space.

This yields geometry-grounded cross-camera IDs at real-time speed without MCBLT's heavy 3D detector.

### Small / long-range object detection

Small/long-range detection is hard because targets occupy few pixels and lose detail under backbone downsampling, and the literature offers three grounded strands. Inference-time slicing — SAHI (Akyon et al., IEEE ICIP 2022) — partitions high-resolution frames into overlapping patches, detects per patch, and merges, lifting AP by ~5–7% inference-only and ~13–15% with slice-aided fine-tuning on VisDrone/xView; it is detector-agnostic and now wired natively into Ultralytics and `supervision`'s `InferenceSlicer`. Architectural tweaks to YOLO add a high-resolution P2 head (stride-4, 160×160) to reach ~4×4-pixel objects plus attentional feature fusion and Soft-NMS — SOD-YOLO (arXiv 2025) reports +20.6% mAP50 and +36.1% mAP50:95 on VisDrone over a YOLOv8 baseline. Efficient transformer detectors form the third strand: RT-DETR (Zhao et al., CVPR 2024) removes NMS and hits 53.0% AP at ~114 FPS (T4), with density-guided variants concentrating compute on dense small-object regions — Dome-DETR (Zhu et al., ACM MM 2025), +3.3 AP on AI-TOD-V2, and ScaleBridge-Det (arXiv, Dec 2025), reported at 35.7% AP on AI-TODv2 with scale-adaptive expert routing.

| Approach | Representative | Gain / result | Cost trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inference slicing | SAHI (ICIP 2022) | +6.8/+5.1/+5.3 AP (FCOS/VFNet/TOOD), inference-only | ~N× forward passes; ~10× for dense grids |
| P2 + fusion YOLO | SOD-YOLO (2025) | +20.6% mAP50, +36.1% mAP50:95 (VisDrone) | Architectural change + retraining |
| NMS-free DETR | RT-DETR (CVPR 2024) | 53.0% AP @ ~114 FPS (T4) | DETR memory footprint |
| Density-guided DETR | Dome-DETR (ACM MM 2025) | +3.3 AP (AI-TOD-V2) at low compute | Adds density extractor |

The SAHI deep-read is unambiguous about the cost model: it adds zero parameters (it wraps the existing weights) so memory is unchanged, but inference scales linearly with slice count — a 1080p frame at 512 px / 0.2 overlap is ~12–15 slices, roughly 10× the per-frame cost, dropping a >100-FPS `yolo11n` to ~5–15 FPS with a dense grid on a consumer GPU and sub-1 FPS on CPU. Slicing also removes global context (hurting large objects unless a full-image pass is added) and can hallucinate small false positives on background, while the largest gains require fine-tuning on sliced data, not just inference.

**Use in this project:** Add a sliced-inference path to `src/models/yolo_wrapper.py`: a `sahi: bool = False` flag (plus `slice_wh=(512,512)`, `overlap=0.2`) in `YOLODetector.__init__`, and a `_detect_sliced()` branch in `detect()` using SAHI's `get_sliced_prediction` over the Ultralytics model (`AutoDetectionModel.from_pretrained(model_type='ultralytics', ...)`). Use a coarse 2×2/2×3 grid, batch slices in one call, and trigger slicing only on a far-field ROI or every Nth frame.

### Cluster insights

- **NMS-free / appearance-free is the unifying edge lever.** The single biggest real-time win across this cluster is removing per-frame, density-dependent compute: NMS-free detection heads (YOLOv10/YOLO26/RT-DETR) make detector latency near-constant regardless of crowd size, and appearance-free association (OC-SORT) makes tracking near-free on top of the detector. The right default for this platform is an NMS-free YOLO + OC-SORT, with heavier appearance/segmentation models (Deep OC-SORT, SAM2MOT) reserved as opt-in.
- **Identity is increasingly an appearance + geometry fusion problem, and both halves are cheap to bolt on.** Single-camera re-entry wants a real embedding (swap the color histogram for OSNet), while cross-camera identity wants metric geometry (a one-time homography to BEV + Hungarian matching) rather than the heavyweight 3D/GNN stacks (MCBLT) whose *principle*, not weights, transfers to edge. Both upgrades land in the existing `src/tracking` and `src/multicam` modules without new model training.
- **Accuracy gains on small/long-range targets are almost always paid in throughput, so they must be opt-in and adaptive.** SAHI tiling and P2 heads materially raise far-field recall but at ~N× cost or a retrain; for a live webcam the only viable posture is full-frame real-time by default with slicing triggered selectively (coarse grid, far-field ROI, every Nth frame) — and the project's own density-heatmap output is the natural signal to drive where that extra compute is spent.

## B. Spatial & Scene Understanding

Where the platform's detection, tracking, and counting stack answers *"what is where in the image plane,"* this cluster pushes toward *"what is where in the scene, and how do the parts relate."* Four sub-domains — promptable segmentation, monocular depth estimation, scene-graph generation, and camera calibration — share a common arc: a heavyweight foundation model defines the accuracy ceiling, and a parallel line of distilled, quantized, or geometrically-grounded variants makes that capability survivable on a consumer webcam or edge box. Together they upgrade the existing pipeline from axis-aligned boxes on a flat frame to pixel-accurate masks, per-pixel ordinal/metric depth, learned object relations, and a calibrated pixel-to-ray mapping that ties multiple cameras into one world frame.

### Promptable segmentation

Promptable segmentation turns a spatial prompt (point/box/mask), a concept prompt (a noun phrase or image exemplar), or both into instance masks. Meta's SAM (2023) established the template — a heavy ViT image encoder, a light prompt encoder, and a fast mask decoder — and **SAM 2** (Ravi et al., ICLR 2025) extended it to video via a streaming-memory transformer: a hierarchical Hiera encoder runs once per frame, while a memory encoder, a FIFO memory bank, and a memory-attention module condition each frame on past predictions so masks propagate forward and backward from a prompt on any frame. SAM 2 reports roughly 6x faster and more accurate operation than SAM on images and around 44 FPS on an A100, matching prior video accuracy (~75 J&F on MOSE) with about 3x fewer interactions. The frontier has since moved to *concept-level* segmentation: **SAM 3** (Meta FAIR, arXiv 2025) adds Promptable Concept Segmentation, detecting, segmenting, and tracking every instance of a text or exemplar concept, reportedly reaching cgF1 54.1 on SA-Co/Gold versus OWLv2's ~24.6 at roughly 30 ms per image for 100+ objects — though, as the deep-read notes, these base models are too heavy for webcam-class hardware.

The deployable line is the efficient-variant family. **FastSAM** (Zhao et al., 2023) reframes the task as YOLOv8-seg all-instance segmentation followed by prompt-guided selection, reporting ~50x speedup over SAM on a single GPU. **MobileSAM** and **EfficientSAM** (Xiong et al., CVPR 2024) distill or pretrain lighter encoders; **EdgeSAM** (Zhou et al., 2023) uses prompt-in-the-loop distillation to clear 30 FPS on an iPhone 14; **EfficientViT-SAM** (Zhang et al., CVPR-W 2024) swaps in a linear-attention backbone for a large measured TensorRT speedup at near-lossless mIoU; and for on-device *video*, **EdgeTAM** (Zhou et al., CVPR 2025) adds a 2D Spatial Perceiver to run SAM 2-style tracking at ~16 FPS on an iPhone 15 Pro Max. The standard SAM 2 deep-read is candid about edge limits: it is real-time only on a strong GPU, is impractical per-frame on CPU/mobile, tracks one object per memory state (multi-object needs multiple passes), and can lose objects across shot changes, long occlusions, fine structures, or fast motion, with memory-bank growth adding latency on long streams.

### Monocular depth estimation

Monocular depth estimation (MDE) predicts a per-pixel depth map from a single RGB frame, in either *affine-invariant relative* depth (closer/farther) or *metric* depth (meters). The field is now dominated by ViT/DINOv2-backbone foundation models trained on large mixes of synthetic and pseudo-labeled real data. For relative depth, **Depth Anything V2** (Yang et al., NeurIPS 2024) is the practical SOTA and the right pick for this platform. Its deep-read describes a teacher–student recipe: train the largest teacher (ViT-G) only on synthetic images for pixel-perfect, sharp depth, then have it pseudo-label 62M+ unlabeled real images to train smaller ViT-S/B/L students — combining synthetic sharpness with real-world generalization. It ships four scales (24.8M / 97.5M / 335.3M / 1.3B), runs over 10x faster than diffusion baselines, and the 24.8M ViT-S is the lightest of any candidate here. **Depth Anything 3** (ByteDance Seed, arXiv 2025) unifies mono/stereo/multi-view in a single plain transformer, reporting roughly +25% geometric accuracy over VGGT at ~20 FPS at 768×1024 on a ViT-L.

For metric output, **UniDepth/UniDepthV2** (Piccinelli et al., CVPR 2024) predict metric 3D points directly without known intrinsics via camera-aware conditioning; **Metric3Dv2** (Hu et al., 2024) jointly predicts metric depth and surface normals (trained on 16M images, reported 1st on KITTI and NYU); and Apple's **Depth Pro** (Bochkovskii et al., 2024) produces a 2.25 MP metric map with absolute scale in ~0.3 s on a V100 with built-in focal-length estimation. Metric-finetuned Depth Anything V2 bridges relative-to-metric (reported KITTI AbsRel ~0.074 / δ1 ~0.946 for ViT-L). The V2 deep-read flags the key caveats: default outputs are *relative* inverse depth (scale/shift-ambiguous), so cross-frame absolute thresholds are unreliable without per-frame normalization or calibration; there is no temporal modeling (video flickers, later addressed by **Video Depth Anything**, CVPR 2025); and the high-quality Base/Large/Giant weights are CC-BY-NC-4.0 while *only the Small model is Apache-2.0* — which conveniently aligns with the real-time target.

### Scene-graph generation

Scene-graph generation (SGG) turns an image into a graph of object nodes and subject–predicate–object edges, evaluated mainly on Visual Genome (VG150: 150 objects, 50 predicates) under PredCls/SGCls/SGDet with Recall@K and mean-Recall@K. Classic two-stage models (Neural Motifs, Tang et al., CVPR 2018; VCTree; IMP; GPS-Net) detect with Faster-RCNN then classify all pairwise predicates — accurate but slow and biased toward frequent predicates. The field has shifted to one-stage DETR-style set prediction that emits sparse triplets directly — **RelTR** (Cong et al., TPAMI 2023), **EGTR** (CVPR 2024), **Hydra-SGG** (2024) — and to **Panoptic SGG** (Yang et al., ECCV 2022), which grounds nodes in segmentation masks to include "stuff." Two current frontiers stand out: open-vocabulary SGG via large multimodal models — **OpenPSG** (Zhou et al., ECCV 2024) for open-set panoptic relations, and training-free VLM frameworks such as **Open-World SGG** (Dutta et al., CVPR-W 2025) that infer over unseen object and relation vocabularies with no task-specific training — and real-time, edge-oriented SGG.

The real-time line is the most relevant here. **REACT** (Neau et al., BMVC 2025), built on the MIT-licensed SGG-Benchmark, replaces the two-stage Faster-RCNN detector with a single-stage YOLOv8 backbone (later YOLO11/12, plus open-vocab YOLO-World), which alone cuts detection latency ~85% (~2.75x) while raising detection accuracy, then attaches a lightweight PE-NET-style predicate head over a filtered set of box proposals. An inference-time Dynamic Candidate Selection (DCS) step adaptively prunes candidate pairs, reportedly cutting latency a further ~66.5% on average without accuracy loss. The deep-read is explicit that this is the only candidate engineered for real-time SGG and that it sits on a YOLOv8 backbone this repo already runs. A recurring caveat across all VG-trained SGG applies: absolute recall is modest (SGDet R@50 often 25–35%), predicate vocabularies are closed-set unless paired with an open-vocab detector, long-tailed predicate bias can suppress rare-but-safety-relevant relations, and reported latencies are GPU figures.

### Camera calibration & geometry

Camera calibration recovers intrinsics (focal length, principal point), extrinsics, and lens distortion, mapping pixels to rays for metric geometry. The classical workhorse is Zhang's planar method via OpenCV's `calibrateCamera`, driven by a checkerboard or, increasingly, a **ChArUco** board whose ArUco-tagged corners stay identifiable under partial occlusion and extreme distortion, yielding sub-pixel reprojection error (well under 1 px when views span the full field of view). Brown-Conrady handles mild radial/tangential distortion, while Kannala-Brandt, UCM/EUCM, and division models cover fisheye and wide-angle lenses. The 2023–2026 frontier is *targetless single-image* deep calibration: regress a dense per-pixel intermediate (incidence field, camera rays, or perspective fields), then recover intrinsics in closed form or via RANSAC. **WildCamera** (Zhu et al., NeurIPS 2023) recovers full 4-DoF intrinsics from a learned incidence field plus RANSAC with no calibration object; **GeoCalib** (Veicht et al., ECCV 2024) fuses a learned perspective prior with geometric optimization for roll/pitch/FoV; **DiffCalib** (He et al., AAAI 2025) generates dense incident maps via diffusion, reporting up to ~40% error reduction; and **Deep-BrownConrady** (Chaudhry et al., IEEE Access/arXiv 2025) regresses H-FoV, principal point, and Brown-Conrady coefficients from one image using largely synthetic training.

The deep-read centers on **AnyCalib** (Tirado-Garín & Civera, ICCV 2025), which reframes calibration as dense per-pixel *ray* regression: a DINOv2 ViT-L/14 encoder feeds a lightweight DPT-style decoder predicting a per-pixel FoV field, each ray learned on the sphere manifold via log/exp maps, after which intrinsics for a chosen camera-model family (pinhole, Brown-Conrady, Kannala-Brandt, UCM/EUCM, division) are recovered in closed form. One trained network reportedly serves 14+ model variants — even on cropped/stretched images — and the authors report outperforming GeoCalib, WildCamera, and DroidCalib across several benchmarks despite training on far less data. The crucial deployment insight is that calibration is *not* a per-frame task: the ~300M-param, ~187.8 GMACs backbone cannot sustain 30 FPS (and is far slower on CPU), but intrinsics are static for a fixed lens, so a one-shot startup calibration cached as `K` + distortion coefficients makes the hot loop cheap OpenCV remapping. Two caveats: the deep-read's specific AnyCalib accuracy row could not be verified against arXiv:2503.12701 and the quoted GeoCalib vFoV error of 3.23° appears to be ~3.21° in the source — both should be checked directly — and a model-family must be chosen up front (separate checkpoints for pinhole vs distorted vs edited), with scope limited to intrinsics and per-pixel rays, not extrinsics.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| OpenCV `calibrateCamera` + ChArUco | Zhang planar | no (board) | intrinsics + Brown-Conrady; <1 px reproj. | offline, gold standard |
| WildCamera (NeurIPS 2023) | incidence field + RANSAC | yes | 4-DoF intrinsics | one-shot, GPU |
| GeoCalib (ECCV 2024) | learned prior + geom. opt. | yes | intrinsics + gravity (roll/pitch/FoV) | one-shot, GPU |
| DiffCalib (AAAI 2025, oral) | diffusion incident map + RANSAC | yes | intrinsics (+depth); ~40% lower error | one-shot, heavier |
| AnyCalib (ICCV 2025) | per-pixel ray regression | yes | 14+ camera models, closed-form K | one-shot; ~300M params, GPU-class |

*Deep single-image calibrators are one-shot-per-source, not per-frame; published FPS/latency for these networks is sparse, so edge inference cost is an open question.*

**Use in this project:** Add `src/utils/calibration.py` wrapping AnyCalib with a `CameraCalibrator.calibrate(frame, cam_model=...)` returning `K` + distortion coefficients cached to a per-source JSON sidecar. Wire it into `src/multicam/manager.py`: add optional intrinsics fields to `CameraFeed`, run a lazy one-shot `calibrate()` per camera in `MultiCameraManager.start()` (GPU once), and apply `cv2.initUndistortRectifyMap` once plus `cv2.remap` in `read_all()` so all downstream consumers receive undistorted frames. The highest-value change is replacing the heuristic class-and-confidence association in `find_cross_camera_matches()` with geometry-aware fusion — back-projecting detection centers through each camera's calibrated rays onto a common ground-plane homography — turning guesswork matching into metric cross-camera association. ### Cluster insights

- **A heavy foundation model plus a distilled/quantized sibling is the universal deployment pattern.** In every sub-domain the accuracy ceiling (SAM 2/3, Depth Anything V2-Giant, two-stage SGG, AnyCalib's ViT-L backbone) is GPU-bound, and the webcam-viable path is a smaller variant or export: ViT-S depth and EdgeSAM/EfficientViT-SAM/EdgeTAM run real-time, REACT rides the YOLOv8 backbone already in the repo, and ONNX/OpenVINO-INT8/TensorRT through `src/deployment` is the recurring lever for CPU and Jetson-class hardware.
- **Spend the expensive computation once, not per frame.** Camera intrinsics are static, so AnyCalib runs at startup and the loop pays only cheap `cv2.remap`; depth and scene-graph extraction can run every Nth frame or on keyframes to protect loop FPS; and SAM 2's streaming memory amortizes a one-time prompt across a tracked sequence rather than re-segmenting each frame. Decoupling a slow analysis cadence from the fast detection/tracking cadence is what keeps these capabilities real-time.
- **These four capabilities compose into geometry-aware perception that strengthens the existing stack.** Calibration provides metric pixel-to-ray mapping, depth adds the third dimension per detection, segmentation makes masks pixel-accurate, and scene graphs add relational semantics — and each plugs into anchors already present (`Detection.mask`, `ObjectRelation.relation`, `find_cross_camera_matches`, the BoT-SORT/ByteTrack association in `src/tracking/tracker.py`), so the upgrade is incremental rather than a rewrite. The persistent open risks are metric-scale reliability from uncalibrated consumer webcams and identity stability under occlusion/distractors when streaming-memory segmentation is paired with the platform's multi-object tracker.

## C. Video Intelligence & Anomaly

This cluster covers the temporal and behavioral intelligence layered on top of the platform's per-frame detection stack: recognizing *what people are doing* (skeleton-based action recognition, temporal video understanding), flagging *what is abnormal* (video anomaly detection), estimating *how many* people are present in congestion (crowd counting), reacting to *safety-critical events* (fall detection and PPE compliance), and the classical OpenCV primitives that make all of this affordable in real time on consumer webcams and edge hardware. The recurring tension throughout is the gap between offline benchmark SOTA (large transformers, multi-stream ensembles, per-clip evaluation) and the platform's hard constraint: bounded, constant per-frame cost on a webcam or Jetson-class device, ideally CPU-viable.

### Skeleton-based action recognition

Skeleton-based action recognition classifies human actions from sequences of 2D/3D joint coordinates. Because skeletons are compact, appearance- and background-invariant, and inherently privacy-preserving, they are an ideal substrate for a webcam platform that already extracts COCO-17 keypoints. Two paradigms dominate. Graph convolutional networks (GCNs) model the skeleton as a spatio-temporal graph: ST-GCN set the template, 2s-AGCN (Shi et al., CVPR 2019) added data-driven adaptive topology and a bone stream (~88.5% NTU60 X-Sub), and CTR-GCN (Chen et al., ICCV 2021) refined per-channel topology into a small, efficient backbone that remains a strong baseline. More recent GCN work pushes NTU120 X-Sub past 90% — DeGCN (Myung et al., IEEE TIP 2024) via deformable joint sampling, and ProtoGCN (Liu et al., arXiv 2024 / CVPR 2025) via prototype-based discrimination of fine-grained actions. A second paradigm rasterizes 2D keypoints into stacked Gaussian heatmap volumes processed by a 3D-CNN: PoseConv3D/PoseC3D (Duan et al., CVPR 2022 Oral) is robust to noisy poses and fuses easily with RGB. Transformers have since surged, with SkateFormer (Do & Kim, ECCV 2024) using partition-specific skeletal-temporal attention. NTU RGB+D 60/120 are the canonical benchmarks (cross-subject/cross-view/cross-setup), with NW-UCLA and Kinetics-Skeleton also common.

| Method | Venue | NTU120 X-Sub | NW-UCLA | Size / cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CTR-GCN (joint stream only) | ICCV 2021 | 84.9% | — | ~1.46M params, ~1.97 GFLOPs/stream | ~23 ms/clip (3rd-party GPU timing) |
| CTR-GCN (4-stream ensemble) | ICCV 2021 | 88.9% | SOTA at pub. | ~4× single-stream cost | NTU60 X-Sub 92.4% / X-View 96.8% |
| PoseConv3D / PoseC3D | CVPR 2022 (Oral) | ~86–87% | — | 3D-CNN over heatmap volumes | SOTA on 6/9 benchmarks; robust to noisy poses |
| DeGCN | IEEE TIP 2024 | ~89.9% (X-Sub) / 90.6% (X-Set) | — | favorable accuracy/size | deformable joint sampling |
| ProtoGCN | arXiv 2024 / CVPR 2025 | >90% | — | prototype-based GCN | NTU60 X-Sub >93% |
| SkateFormer | ECCV 2024 | ~89.8% | 98.3% | skeletal-temporal transformer | partition-specific attention |

The deep-read on CTR-GCN makes the case for this platform concretely. Each CTR-GC block learns a *shared* topology as a generic prior plus a small *channel-specific* refinement computed from pairwise joint correlations on transformed features, giving every channel its own dynamic, sample-dependent topology while adding very few parameters over a shared-topology GConv. A single joint stream (~1.46M params, ~1.97 GFLOPs) consumes joint coordinates directly — exactly what YOLOv8-pose already outputs — so there is no heavy heatmap-volume construction. Third-party timing of ~23 ms/clip implies ~40+ FPS on a modern GPU, and related GCNs at this scale reach 140–190 FPS at batch 1 on an RTX 4080. The principal caveats: headline accuracy needs the ~4× 4-stream ensemble; the model is designed for trimmed, pre-segmented clips over a fixed temporal window rather than native streaming onset/offset detection; it was trained on clean 3D (NTU) or curated 2D (NW-UCLA), so accuracy depends heavily on upstream pose quality and degrades on noisy/occluded 2D keypoints — the exact weakness PoseConv3D targets. CPU/edge real-time for a single stream is plausible but is *not* benchmarked in the paper, so it should be treated as uncertain pending ONNX-INT8 or a lighter "fast baseline" variant.

**Use in this project:** Replace the brittle rule-based heuristics in `src/models/pose_detector.py` (the `ActionClassifier`) with a learned single-stream CTR-GCN head in a new `src/models/ctrgcn.py`, exported to ONNX via `src/deployment/exporter.py`; in `src/analytics/temporal.py` add a per-track keypoint ring buffer that accumulates ~64 normalized COCO-17 frames from `PoseResult.keypoints`, and when a window fills, write the predicted action + confidence back into `PoseResult.action`/`action_confidence` so `scripts/run_action.py`'s existing draw/label loop works unchanged. Expose a `--classifier {rules,ctrgcn}` flag (rules kept as the no-dependency fallback) and a `--window-size` arg, running the single joint stream to stay within the latency budget.

### Video anomaly detection

Video anomaly detection identifies events deviating from learned "normal" behavior, evaluated mainly by frame-level ROC-AUC. The field splits into three regimes. Unsupervised/one-class methods train only on normal video via reconstruction or future-frame prediction: memory-augmented autoencoders (MNAD; Park et al., CVPR 2020) and self-supervised pretext tasks dominate, with Jigsaw (Wang et al., ECCV 2022) reaching ~84.3% AUC on ShanghaiTech and near-99% on Ped2 by solving decoupled spatio-temporal puzzles, and diffusion models (MA-PDM; Zhou et al., AAAI 2025) now giving sharper normal reconstructions and modest AUC gains over prior diffusion baselines. Weakly-supervised VAD uses only video-level labels via multiple-instance learning on UCF-Crime and XD-Violence: RTFM (ICCV 2021) was a strong MIL baseline, and CLIP/vision-language adaptation now leads — VadCLIP (Wu et al., AAAI 2024) reaches 88.02% AUC on UCF-Crime, and πVAD (Majhi et al., CVPR 2025) pushes to ~90.33% by inducing pose/depth/flow/panoptic/language cues that are used only at training, keeping inference RGB-only. The newest frontier is training-free, zero-shot detection with multimodal LLMs (HiProbe-VAD, EventVAD, SlowFastVAD, MoniTor; 2025–2026) that caption or probe hidden states for interpretable, open-set reasoning. For real-time edge deployment, lightweight dual-branch and object-centric pipelines trade a few AUC points for speed.

| Method | Venue | Regime | Headline AUC | Real-time fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MNAD (prediction) | CVPR 2020 | Unsupervised, per-scene | Ped2 97.0% / Avenue 88.5% / ShanghaiTech 70.5% | ~67 FPS on 2017-class GPU; no optical flow |
| Jigsaw | ECCV 2022 | Self-supervised, unsupervised | ShanghaiTech ~84.3% / Ped2 ~99% | self-supervised pretext, no anomaly labels |
| RTFM | ICCV 2021 | Weakly-supervised (MIL) | ShanghaiTech ~97.2% | frozen I3D features + MIL head |
| VadCLIP | AAAI 2024 | Weakly-supervised (CLIP) | UCF-Crime 88.02% / XD-Violence 84.51% AP | dual-branch CLIP |
| πVAD | CVPR 2025 | Weakly-supervised, multi-modal train | UCF-Crime ~90.33% | RGB-only at inference |
| MLLM zero-shot (HiProbe-VAD etc.) | 2025–2026 | Training-free | ShanghaiTech ~85% | interpretable; heavier, async-only |

The deep-read on MNAD is the most actionable for this platform. MNAD wraps a U-Net-style encoder-decoder with a learnable memory module of a small set of prototype items (~10 in the paper) that record diverse normal patterns; encoder features query the memory via attention, read-out items are concatenated with the query and decoded, and two losses (feature compactness and separateness) keep features and prototypes discriminative. The anomaly score combines prediction/reconstruction error (PSNR) with the query-to-nearest-prototype distance, and no optical flow is used — the memory module itself adds roughly +2.7% to +4.0% AUC over a no-memory baseline. The reported ~67 FPS on a GTX TITAN Xp means a modern consumer GPU should comfortably clear 30 FPS, and the existing ONNX/TensorRT path can accelerate it further; CPU-only real-time at full resolution is unlikely (a few FPS), but low-resolution, grayscale, or every-Nth-frame scoring is viable on edge. The real catch is not inference cost but the per-scene assumption: the model must be trained or fine-tuned on a short clip of each camera's normal footage, it does not transfer to a new viewpoint without re-fitting, the score is an uncalibrated PSNR-plus-distance value needing a per-camera threshold, and ShanghaiTech's 70.5% (vs Ped2's 97.0%) shows it weakens on crowded multi-scene data. It detects appearance/motion deviation, not semantic events. (Exact parameter count, input resolution, and memory-item count were not stated in the sources fetched and should be treated as unconfirmed.)

**Use in this project:** Add a pixel-level anomaly backend alongside the existing detection-metadata `IsolationForest`. Create `src/analytics/mnad_anomaly.py` implementing an `MNADAnomalyDetector` with the same `update()`/`fit()`/`check()` interface as `src/analytics/anomaly_detector.py` (so the app and Streamlit dashboard are unchanged), wrapping the MNAD prediction encoder-decoder + memory module in torch; add `scripts/train_mnad.py` to learn normal patterns from a short per-source clip and save weights + memory items; and extend `scripts/run_anomaly.py` with `--backend {isoforest,mnad}` plus `--mnad-weights`. Fire `src/alerts.py` on a smoothed PSNR-plus-memory-distance score above a per-camera threshold, keeping IsolationForest as the zero-training default. As a longer-horizon option, a training-free MLLM detector could run as an asynchronous verifier on flagged clips for interpretable open-set reasoning without touching the per-frame budget.

### Temporal video understanding

Temporal video understanding has bifurcated into two regimes. Offline, transformer-based video foundation models dominate: VideoMAE V2 (Wang et al., CVPR 2023) scales masked-autoencoder pretraining to a billion-parameter ViT-g, reaching 90.0% top-1 on Kinetics-400 and 77.0% on Something-Something V2, and its frozen features now back most temporal-action-localization (TAL) pipelines. Online/streaming detection, which is what matters for a live webcam, prioritizes constant per-frame cost. TeSTra (Zhao & Krähenbühl, ECCV 2022) reformulates cross-attention as a temporal-smoothing kernel for streaming inference at >600 FPS; MiniROAD (An et al., ICCV 2023) shows a minimal GRU with non-uniform loss weighting reaches 71.8% mAP on THUMOS'14 at a fraction of transformer cost; HAT (Reza et al., ECCV 2024) adds long-term history to anchors for online TAL; and VLM-driven TOAD (arXiv 2025) uses CLIP text embeddings to push THUMOS'14 to ~82.46% mAP. For edge hardware, MoViNet stream buffers (CVPR 2021) and EdgeOAR's early-exit fusion (Luo et al., arXiv 2024 / IEEE TMC 2025) cut latency and energy by very large margins versus heavy baselines. The 2025–2026 frontier is streaming video-LLMs with compressive long-term memory (ProVideLLM, StreamingVLM) sustaining 10–25 FPS within ~2 GB of GPU memory; reported peak figures for these very recent systems should be read as indicative rather than settled.

| Method | Venue | Task | Accuracy | Cost / speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| VideoMAE V2 (ViT-g) | CVPR 2023 | Offline classification / TAL features | K400 90.0% / SSv2 77.0% | billion-param; offline only |
| TeSTra | ECCV 2022 | Online detection | THUMOS'14 ~59.9% mAP (RGB) | >600 FPS, ~6× faster than sliding-window |
| MiniROAD | ICCV 2023 | Online action detection | THUMOS'14 71.8% mAP / TVSeries 89.6% mcAP | ~10.5M params, ~1.08 GFLOPs |
| HAT | ECCV 2024 | Online TAL | SOTA on egocentric PREGO | history-augmented anchors |
| TOAD | arXiv 2025 | Online detection (VLM) | THUMOS'14 82.46% mAP | CLIP text embeddings, low overhead |
| EdgeOAR | arXiv 2024 / TMC 2025 | Edge online recognition | UCF-101 | up to ~99% latency/energy reduction |

The deep-read on MiniROAD diagnoses the RNN accuracy gap as a train/inference mismatch: training supervises every timestep equally, but inference only uses the latest timestep, so early timesteps inject noisy gradients. The fix is a non-uniform loss weighting that down-weights early timesteps. The model is deliberately minimal — a single-layer GRU (hidden 1024) consuming one precomputed clip feature per frame, carrying all history in its hidden state at O(1) per-frame cost, with no transformer, memory bank, or sliding window (~10.5M params, ~1.08 GFLOPs vs ~12.9M / ~1.96G for a comparable transformer; the GRU runs ~200× faster than prior OAD heads on an RTX 3090). The decisive caveat is that the *published* pipeline depends on heavy two-stream I3D features plus optical flow, so the genuine bottleneck is upstream feature extraction, not the GRU — the headline FPS isolates the recurrent head. Running this on a webcam in real time means dropping optical flow and replacing I3D with a lightweight RGB clip encoder (X3D-XS / MobileNetV3, or reusing CNN features the YOLO stack already computes) and retraining the GRU with the non-uniform loss; expect some accuracy drop from dropping flow, and note that accuracy is reported only with Kinetics I3D features and that this is frame-level "what is happening now," not precise start/end localization, over a fixed vocabulary.

**Use in this project:** `src/analytics/temporal.py` is today purely heuristic/geometric (loitering, abandoned-object, crowd, pixel-displacement speed) with no learned temporal model. Add a `MiniRoadHead` class wrapping a single-layer GRU (hidden 1024) that persists its hidden state across frames, mirroring the existing `TemporalBuffer.add()` / `EventDetector.check()` loop: per frame, extract a lightweight RGB clip embedding (reuse the torch backbone or a small X3D/MobileNet), GRU-step it, and surface high-confidence transitions as a new `EventType.ACTION_RECOGNIZED` `TemporalEvent` flowing into the alert system and dashboard. Ship a small trainer under `src/training/` applying MiniROAD's non-uniform per-timestep loss, with optical flow optional and off by default. The open architectural question for this module is the right *bounded* long-term memory so multi-minute events persist at constant per-frame cost — bridging the current fixed-length deque and 2025 compressive-streaming-memory approaches.

### Crowd counting & density estimation

Crowd counting estimates the number of people even in congested scenes where individual detection fails — precisely where the platform's YOLO person path breaks down. The field has converged on three paradigms: (1) density-map regression, where a CNN regresses a per-pixel density map whose integral is the count, with CSRNet (Li et al., CVPR 2018) and its dilated convolutions a long-standing baseline (ShanghaiTech A MAE ~68); (2) point-/localization-based methods — P2PNet (Song et al., ICCV 2021 Oral) and PET (Liu et al., ICCV 2023) — that directly predict head coordinates via Hungarian one-to-one matching, jointly giving counts and per-person localization; and (3) loss-driven approaches like DM-Count (Wang et al., NeurIPS 2020) that replace Gaussian-smoothed targets with optimal-transport distribution matching. The 2023–2025 SOTA is scale-aware and transformer/VLM-based: STEERER (Han et al., ICCV 2023) resolves scale variation via selective inheritance learning, and CLIP-EBC (Ma et al., arXiv 2024) reframes counting as enhanced blockwise classification on frozen CLIP, reaching NWPU-Crowd test MAE 58.2. A parallel thrust targets edge deployment via lightweight CNNs, MobileViT decoders, Mamba/state-space backbones, and distillation (e.g. sub-1M-param models), trading a few MAE points for high-FPS inference.

| Method | Venue | ShanghaiTech A MAE | Other | Size / speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CSRNet | CVPR 2018 | ~68 | — | dilated-CNN baseline |
| DM-Count | NeurIPS 2020 | large-margin gains over prior SOTA | strong on UCF-QNRF/NWPU | optimal-transport loss |
| P2PNet | ICCV 2021 (Oral) | ~52.7 | Part B ~6.25; UCF-QNRF ~85.3 | ~21.8M params (VGG16-bn), ~94.5 FPS GPU |
| PET | ICCV 2023 | SOTA counting + localization | — | point-query quadtree |
| STEERER | ICCV 2023 | — | prev. best NWPU; ShanghaiTech B MAE ~5.8 | HRNet-based, ~17 FPS |
| CLIP-EBC | arXiv 2024 | — | NWPU MAE 58.2 / RMSE 268.5 | frozen CLIP backbone |

The deep-read on P2PNet shows why it slots cleanly into this platform's analytics seam. P2PNet directly predicts a set of point proposals matching the raw dot annotations: a VGG16-bn backbone with an upsampling neck produces dense anchor points, two branches regress per-point coordinate offsets and confidence, training matches predictions one-to-one to ground truth via the Hungarian algorithm (cost combining pixel distance and confidence), and at inference points above a confidence threshold give both the count and the localizations. It also introduces density-normalized average precision (nAP). At ~21.8M params it runs ~94.5 FPS (~21.8 ms/frame) on a desktop GPU — far above VGG-density methods (~23–28 FPS) and HRNet-based STEERER (~17 FPS) — and comfortably 30+ FPS on consumer RTX-class GPUs at 720p–1080p; CPU-only is a few FPS at full resolution, so the edge recipe is downscale, run every Nth frame, and export to ONNX/INT8 TensorRT. Crucially, unlike YOLO it keeps working in dense scenes (dozens-to-hundreds of heads), so it *complements* rather than replaces the detector. Caveats: it is trained/evaluated on still images, so per-frame points flicker and carry no IDs (needs an external tracker for trajectories); the confidence threshold trades precision vs recall per scene; it degrades on tiny/blurred far-field heads and heavy occlusion; and reported FPS is server-GPU, with no CPU/edge latency in the paper.

**Use in this project:** Create `src/models/p2pnet_wrapper.py` wrapping the official VGG16-bn P2PNet weights and returning the repo's `Detection` objects with zero-area boxes centered on each head point plus a `crowd` class and confidence. Add `scripts/run_crowd_counter.py` mirroring `scripts/run_heatmap.py`: stream frames, overlay the live count, and feed the point `Detection`s straight into the existing `HeatmapGenerator` (`src/analytics/heatmap.py`, which already accumulates detection centers) so dense-crowd heatmaps stop undercounting where YOLO fails. The same points drop into `src/analytics/zone_counter.py` for true per-zone occupancy in congested areas and into `src/alerts.py` for crowd-density thresholds, with an ONNX/TensorRT path via `scripts/export_model.py` + `scripts/benchmark.py`. Transfer to a fixed indoor viewpoint without on-site labels remains uncertain; the project's active-learning loop is the natural place to bootstrap cheap pseudo-point annotations.

### Classical OpenCV pipelines

Classical OpenCV primitives — optical flow, background subtraction, feature matching, contour analysis, Kalman filtering — remain the practical backbone of real-time CV on consumer webcams and edge devices, where their low latency, zero training cost, and CPU-friendliness still beat deep nets for cheap motion and foreground cues. The 2023–2026 frontier is a convergence in which lightweight learned models now match classical speed on commodity hardware. SEA-RAFT (Wang et al., ECCV 2024 Oral) and NeuFlow v2 (Zhang et al., arXiv 2024) bring accurate optical flow to >20 FPS on a Jetson Orin Nano (10–70× speedups), while XFeat (Potje et al., CVPR 2024) runs robust local-feature matching in real time on a laptop CPU, up to ~5× faster than SuperPoint. For foreground masks, MOG2/KNN stay the default fast option; deep methods (FgSegNet variants) reach ~0.98 F-measure on CDnet2014 but rarely fit a real-time edge budget. Benchmarks consistently show ORB/SIFT trailing learned matchers (SuperPoint/ALIKED + LightGlue (Lindenberger et al., ICCV 2023), LoFTR) in accuracy yet winning on raw runtime. The dominant deployment pattern is therefore hybrid: classical motion/ROI gating to trigger selective deep inference. OpenCV 5.0 (2026) is the platform-level shift, with a rewritten CPU DNN engine (ONNX coverage from ~22% to >80%) that the project's import/export stack can target directly.

| Method | Venue | Role | Speed | Accuracy notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MOG2 / KNN | OpenCV | Background subtraction | real-time on CPU (baseline) | fast; FgSegNet ~0.98 F-measure but not real-time on edge |
| ORB / SIFT | OpenCV | Handcrafted features | fastest raw runtime | trail learned matchers in accuracy |
| XFeat (sparse) | CVPR 2024 | Learned features (CPU) | 27.1 FPS @ VGA on i5-1135G7; ~1400 FPS RTX 4090 | AUC@5/10/20 ≈ 42.6/56.4/67.7, ≈ SuperPoint |
| SuperPoint | — | Learned features | 3.0 FPS @ VGA (same CPU) | strong, but ~9× slower than XFeat on CPU |
| SEA-RAFT | ECCV 2024 (Oral) | Optical flow | ≥2.3× faster than RAFT-class | 3.69 EPE on Spring (22.9% error reduction) |
| NeuFlow v2 | arXiv 2024 | Edge optical flow | >20 FPS @ 512×384 on Orin Nano | 10–70× speedup at comparable accuracy |

The deep-read on XFeat is the standout for a no-GPU-required goal. From a single forward pass XFeat predicts three maps at 1/8 resolution — a keypoint heatmap, a dense 64-D descriptor map, and a reliability heatmap — using a featherweight backbone with aggressive early downsampling and tripling channels as resolution drops. Sparse mode matches the compact 64-D descriptors via mutual nearest neighbors; XFeat* adds an efficient semi-dense mode with a lightweight match-refinement module instead of an expensive transformer. Trained on MegaDepth plus synthetic homographic warps of COCO, it is the rare learned detector+descriptor that hits real time on a plain laptop CPU: 27.1 FPS sparse / 19.2 FPS semi-dense at VGA on an i5-1135G7 with vanilla PyTorch (vs SuperPoint 3.0 / DISK 1.2), ~1,400 FPS sparse on an RTX 4090, depends only on torch (OpenCV for I/O), and is Apache-2.0 with community ONNX/TensorRT exports. Caveats: accuracy still trails the heaviest semi-dense/dense matchers (LoFTR, ASpanFormer, DISK+LightGlue), raw mutual-NN matching needs RANSAC/geometric verification (the authors pair it with a "LighterGlue" variant for best pose), and the 27 FPS figure is VGA-specific — higher resolutions, low-texture/repetitive scenes, and Python NMS post-processing degrade CPU throughput.

**Use in this project:** Add `src/utils/features.py` wrapping XFeat (load via `torch.hub` `verlab/accelerated_features`; `detectAndCompute` → keypoints + 64-D descriptors; `match()` doing mutual-NN + `cv2.findHomography` RANSAC) as an OpenCV-style alternative to ORB/SIFT, and wire it into two weak spots: (1) `src/multicam/manager.py` `find_cross_camera_matches` — replace the class-id + confidence-difference heuristic with a real inter-camera homography/fundamental matrix estimated from XFeat matches to confirm the same physical object across overlapping views; and (2) `src/tracking/tracker.py` `_extract_embedding` — augment the color-histogram re-ID with XFeat keypoint matching between a track's stored crop and re-entry candidates. It also enables a new `src/utils/stabilize.py` computing the homography between consecutive `src/stream.py` frames for camera-motion compensation, stabilizing density heatmaps and cutting false anomaly/line-crossing triggers on shaky feeds. The broader design question for this module is adaptive hybrid gating — using cheap classical motion/flow cues to decide *when* to invoke YOLO/OWLv2/pose per-frame and per-ROI — and whether the OpenCV 5 CPU DNN engine shifts the classical-vs-learned crossover.

### Fall detection & workplace safety

Vision-based fall detection and workplace-safety CV have converged on a two-stage recipe: a person/pose detector front-end (YOLO-pose, MediaPipe, RTMPose) feeding a temporal classifier. For falls, skeleton graph models lead on accuracy — a three-stream spatial-temporal GCN with adaptive feature aggregation (Sania et al., arXiv 2408.12211, 2024; later Scientific Reports 2025) reports 98.97–99.97% across UR-Fall, UP-Fall, ImViA/Le2i, and FU-Kinect — while lighter pipelines pair pose keypoints with LSTM/Transformer or classical ML. The 2025 trend is edge-first, privacy-preserving deployment on Jetson-class and CPU-only hardware: ElderFallGuard (arXiv 2505.11845, 2025) runs MediaPipe + Random Forest with prone-pose-plus-motion-drop logic and Telegram alerts, and dedicated CPU pose-fall systems (Mali & Jaiswal, arXiv 2503.19501, 2025) target consumer webcams. For workplace PPE/hard-hat compliance, the field is almost entirely YOLO-based detection with paired positive/negative classes (helmet/no-helmet, vest/no-vest): YOLOv8-CGS (PLOS One 2025) reports 93.18% mAP@0.5 at ~89 FPS and GCB-YOLOv11 ~93.6% mAP at ~90.3 FPS, while new datasets — SH17 (Ahmad & Rahimi, arXiv 2407.04590, 2024; 8,099 images, 17 classes, YOLOv9-e >70.9% mAP) and Ultralytics Construction-PPE — standardize evaluation. Reported benchmark accuracy is high but inflated by staged datasets; real-world false-alarm reduction, occlusion, and cross-domain generalization remain open, and privacy (skeleton-only or on-device processing) is now a first-class design constraint.

| ElderFallGuard | arXiv 2025 | Fall (pose + RF) | 100% on custom 7,200-sample set (staged) | edge; prone+motion-drop rule |
| Pose-Based Fall (CPU) | arXiv 2503.19501, 2025 | Fall (pose + voting) | acc. 86.86% / prec. 85.23% / recall 93.75% / F1 93.17% on GMDCSA-24 | standard CPU, no GPU |
| YOLOv8-CGS | PLOS One 2025 | Helmet compliance | 93.18% mAP@0.5, ~89 FPS | real-time detection |
| GCB-YOLOv11 | 2025 | Helmet compliance | ~93.6% mAP, ~90.3 FPS | real-time detection |
| YOLOv9-e on SH17 | arXiv 2024 | 17-class PPE | >70.9% mAP | manufacturing PPE |

The deep-read on the CPU pose-fall system (Mali & Jaiswal, arXiv 2503.19501) is the closest match to the platform's existing pose path: MediaPipe Pose / BlazePose landmarks plus six geometric features and threshold voting over a 20-frame buffer, sensor-free and CPU real-time. On the GMDCSA-24 dataset it reports accuracy 86.86%, precision 85.23%, recall 93.75%, and F1 93.17%; the BlazePose backbone is a single-digit-MB on-device model that comfortably hits ~20–30 FPS on consumer laptop CPUs, and the fall logic (per-frame geometry + a small ring buffer) is negligible cost. The paper emphasizes CPU real-time operation but does not publish an explicit FPS/latency figure or a measured parameter/memory count, so the throughput claim is qualitative. Its limitations are characteristic of the whole hand-crafted-threshold family: parameters were chosen empirically and may not generalize across camera angle, mounting height, occlusion, or lighting; it is single-person/monocular-geometry oriented and degrades when landmarks are occluded or the subject is far/partially out of frame; and learned ST-GCN methods report substantially higher accuracy. A platform-level caution applies across this whole sub-domain: the headline 99–100% figures come from staged, balanced datasets, so real-world robustness against genuine falls, occlusion, multiple people, low light, and extreme class imbalance is unproven.

**Use in this project:** `src/models/pose_detector.py`'s `ActionClassifier` already mirrors this paper but in a weaker form — a single crude torso-angle < 30° threshold over a 15-frame deque. Upgrade it to the multi-feature voting scheme: add a `FallDetector` that, per `track_id`, computes the six features (height/aspect ratio, torso-leg angle, knee-ankle position, head distance from frame-bottom, upper-body alignment, hip-movement speed), thresholds each, and accumulates votes over a 20-frame buffer (raise `sequence_length` 15 → 20) before emitting a high-confidence "fall" only when votes persist; wire the confirmed event into `src/alerts.py` (track_id, bbox, feature snapshot) for the alert system and dashboard. This adds debounced, multi-cue fall detection at near-zero added compute. PPE compliance is a natural companion module — a YOLO detector with paired helmet/vest classes feeding the same alert path — though whether a single open-vocabulary/VLM detector can generalize PPE across sites and gear types without per-site retraining remains an open question, as does the privacy choice between skeleton-only transmission and on-device-only inference.

### Cluster insights

- **The recurring blocker is the feature/perception front-end, not the temporal head.** Across MiniROAD (cheap GRU bottlenecked by two-stream I3D + optical flow), CTR-GCN (tiny GCN gated by upstream pose quality), and P2PNet (compact head but server-GPU FPS only), the lightweight temporal/learned model is rarely the cost or accuracy ceiling — the upstream encoder is. This argues for a shared, real-time perception backbone (the existing YOLO-pose keypoints, a lightweight RGB clip encoder, reused CNN features) feeding multiple cheap O(1)-state heads, rather than a separate heavy extractor per task.

- **Per-scene adaptation and threshold calibration are unavoidable for unsupervised/heuristic behavior models on arbitrary webcams.** MNAD must be trained on each camera's normal footage and emits an uncalibrated score; the CPU fall detector and the existing temporal heuristics rely on hand-tuned thresholds that may not transfer; benchmark AUC/accuracy (often inflated by staged or single-scene data — ShanghaiTech 70.5% vs Ped2 97.0%, 99–100% staged falls) does not predict in-the-wild behavior. The platform should treat short on-device "normal" learning windows, per-camera thresholds, and the active-learning loop as core infrastructure, not afterthoughts.

- **Classical and learned methods are complements, and a hybrid gating architecture is the unifying real-time strategy.** Classical OpenCV primitives (MOG2/KNN, XFeat-class matching, Kalman gating) provide cheap, training-free motion/foreground/correspondence cues that can decide *when* to invoke the expensive paths (YOLO, OWLv2, pose, MNAD, crowd counting) per-frame and per-ROI; point-based crowd counting fills the dense-scene gap where YOLO fails; and skeleton/pose substrates double as the privacy-preserving layer. Maximizing sustained FPS on consumer/edge hardware is therefore less about any single SOTA model and more about adaptive orchestration across these layers — a direction the imminent OpenCV 5 CPU DNN engine may further shift.

## D. Models, Data & Deployment

This cluster covers the pieces that turn a research-grade detector into a deployable product: how models are compressed and run on edge accelerators, how training data is acquired efficiently (active learning) or manufactured (generative augmentation), how identity is removed for compliance, and how a single self-supervised backbone can amortize features across the platform's many heads. The unifying theme is that real-time CV on consumer webcams is now an *inference- and data-engineering* discipline as much as an architecture one.

### Edge deployment & model optimization

The state of the art treats real-time edge inference as a joint optimization of the accuracy–latency–power envelope for a *specific* target device, validated by on-device benchmarking rather than FLOP counts. The canonical pipeline is: train in PyTorch, export to a hardware-matched runtime (TensorRT for NVIDIA/Jetson, ONNX Runtime as the portable default, OpenVINO for Intel CPU/NPU, NCNN/TFLite for ARM and mobile), then compress via quantization, pruning, and distillation. INT8 post-training quantization (PTQ) with a small calibration set is the single highest-leverage step, typically yielding 1.5–3.3× speedups (Hidayatullah et al., arXiv 2025); the same survey cautions that *poor* calibration can cost roughly 3–7 absolute mAP50-95, with the smallest models hit hardest, so quantization-aware training or careful calibration is the mitigation. The 2024–2026 detector frontier is co-designed for this regime: YOLO11 and the NMS-free YOLO26 (Sapkota et al., arXiv 2025) deliberately remove quantization-hostile components such as Distribution Focal Loss so that INT8 exports retain near-FP32 accuracy, while transformer detectors now reach real-time speed — RT-DETR-L attains 53.1% AP at 108 FPS on a T4 (Zhao, Lv et al., CVPR 2024). Complementary compression is mature: structured channel pruning plus channel-wise distillation cuts a YOLOv8m by ~73.5% of its parameters for only −2.7 AP50 (Shokouhmand et al., arXiv 2025). A broad survey of embedded DL infrastructure frames these as recurring "pillars" spanning CNNs to ViTs (Liu et al., ACM TECS / arXiv 2024).

A deep read of YOLO26 (Sapkota et al., arXiv 2025) is the most directly actionable result for this platform. By emitting a compact, non-redundant prediction set (true end-to-end NMS-free decoding) and dropping DFL in favor of a direct box parameterization, exported graphs map cleanly to ONNX/TensorRT/CoreML/TFLite with fewer custom kernels, and — critically for edge — the model stays stable under INT8 where RT-DETR-style detectors reportedly degrade sharply. NMS-free decoding also removes a variable post-processing cost, giving steadier per-frame latency for streaming. The trade-offs to keep in mind: the headline numbers are largely vendor/Ultralytics-reported rather than independent reproductions, NMS-free mode may trade a small amount of recall on dense/overlapping scenes, and the larger m/l/x scales remain far from real-time on CPU without GPU/NPU acceleration.

### Active learning for detection

Active learning (AL) aims to reach a target mAP using a small fraction of labeled data by iteratively querying the most informative images or boxes. The field organizes around two complementary axes — uncertainty (informativeness) and diversity (representativeness) — and detection is harder than classification because each image mixes localization and classification difficulty across a variable number of instances, making image-level score aggregation non-trivial. Task-agnostic anchors remain widely used as baselines: Learning Loss adds an auxiliary loss-prediction module (Yoo & Kweon, CVPR 2019), Core-Set frames selection as geometric coverage (ICLR 2018), and BADGE seeds k-means++ over hallucinated gradient embeddings to capture uncertainty and diversity simultaneously with no hyperparameter tuning (Ash et al., ICLR 2020). Detection-specific work moved to the instance level with MI-AOD's multiple-instance, adversarial classifier-discrepancy re-weighting (Yuan, Wan, Wang et al., CVPR 2021). The current strong, detector-agnostic baseline is PPAL (Yang, Pang, Crowley et al., CVPR 2024), which fuses difficulty-calibrated uncertainty with category-conditioned diversity. The newest direction is performance-guided reinforcement learning that selects data to directly maximize expected mAP (Liang et al., arXiv 2026); note this is a recent, not-yet-independently-corroborated result and its "highest accuracy curve" claim should be treated as preliminary. Reported efficiency is consistent rather than spectacular: AL typically matches random-sampling accuracy with roughly 50–60% fewer labels (e.g., ~50% on LiDAR detection, arXiv 2025), and the often-quoted "5–10×" labeling efficiency appears to hold mainly in easier or class-imbalanced regimes — for small, narrow-domain webcam detectors the realistic figure is plausibly closer to ~2×.

A deep read of PPAL clarifies how to upgrade a heuristic sampler cheaply. Its two stages are Difficulty-Calibrated Uncertainty Sampling (per-instance uncertainty re-weighted by a category-wise coefficient fusing classification and localization difficulty, so rare/hard classes survive ranking) followed by Category-Conditioned Matching Similarity (image-to-image similarity as an ensemble of category-conditioned instance matches, then k-Means++ over the candidate pool for a diverse batch). It uses only off-the-shelf detector outputs and changes neither architecture nor training, so it inherits the speed of whatever detector you plug in — it never runs on the live stream, only between training rounds. Caveats: it was evaluated on RetinaNet and Faster R-CNN (not modern YOLO/DETR), its category-wise difficulty coefficients assume a fixed known label set (a poor fit for the open-vocabulary OWLv2 path), and a 2026 follow-up reports it needing ~20.7% more annotations than newer reinforced selection — strong but not final SOTA.

|---|---|---|---|
| Learning Loss (CVPR 2019) | Uncertainty | Yes | Beats prior AL across cls/det/pose |
| Core-Set (ICLR 2018) | Diversity | Yes | Geometric-coverage baseline |
| BADGE (ICLR 2020) | Both | Yes | Matches/beats peers, no tuning |

### Generative / synthetic data augmentation

Synthetic augmentation has shifted from classical geometric/photometric transforms toward generative pipelines that synthesize new, label-consistent training images, with text-to-image diffusion (Stable Diffusion 1.5/2, SDXL) as the engine — steered by ControlNet (edges, depth, pose, layout) and LoRA/textual-inversion personalization so synthetic objects match a target domain and yield free, accurate boxes or masks. Three families lead. Image-editing augmentation semantically perturbs few-shot examples — DA-Fusion adds up to +10 points few-shot accuracy on Pascal/COCO-derived tasks (Trabucco et al., ICLR 2024). Annotation-from-generation decodes a diffusion model's internal features into perception labels from ~1% (~100) labeled images, giving +12.1% AP for COCO instance segmentation (Wu et al., DatasetDM, NeurIPS 2023). Generative copy-paste and layout-controlled synthesis target detection and the long tail: X-Paste adds up to +6.8 box AP on LVIS long-tail classes (Zhao et al., ICML 2023), and ODGEN reports up to +25.3% mAP@.50:.95 on YOLOv5/v7 (Zhu et al., NeurIPS 2024); a controllable-diffusion plus category-calibrated-CLIP filtering pipeline improves few-shot MSCOCO/PASCAL detection (Fang et al., ControlAug, WACV 2024). The open frontier is the diversity–faithfulness–efficiency trade-off: naive 10–30× synthetic expansion adds compute without robust gains, so 2025–2026 work emphasizes targeted/curriculum augmentation, OOD post-filtering, and CLIP-calibrated selection to generate fewer, higher-value samples. Because generation is offline, it complements rather than burdens real-time edge inference.

A deep read of ODGEN (Zhu et al., NeurIPS 2024) shows both the power and the cost. It domain-adapts Stable Diffusion by fine-tuning the UNet on *both* cropped foreground objects and full scenes, generates images conditioned on a bbox layout via composited visual prompts plus per-box class text, and then audits each labeled region with a foreground/background discriminator that filters pseudo-labels where no synthetic object is detected — cleaning the annotations before mixing with real data. The headline +25.3% gain is measured by adding 5,000 synthetic images to a 200-real-image baseline across seven Roboflow-100 domains. Crucially, this is an offline *dataset factory*, not an on-device model: it needs per-domain UNet fine-tuning and thousands of slow denoising passes, realistically requiring V100/A100-class GPUs (per the FLORA follow-up), and quality/box-faithfulness still degrade on dense, occluded, many-class scenes. Its efficiency is also not final — FLORA reports beating the ODGEN baseline with 500 vs 5,000 synthetic images.

| X-Paste (ICML 2023) | Long-tail instance seg | +6.8 box / +6.5 mask AP (LVIS tail) | Offline copy-paste + SD/CLIP |

### Privacy-preserving vision

Privacy-preserving vision has shifted from non-realistic obfuscation (blur, pixelation, blackout) toward realistic generative anonymization that removes identity while preserving downstream utility such as pose, expression, and detection/tracking signals; surveys taxonomize methods into pixel-, representation-, and semantic-level and show that simple blur/pixelation both cripple analytics and are often reversible (Face De-identification survey, arXiv 2024). The GAN-era anchor is DeepPrivacy2, a style-based full-body and face anonymizer requiring no pose guidance (Hukkelås & Lindseth, WACV 2023). The 2024–2026 frontier is diffusion-based and these are recent, largely single-paper results to be read as promising rather than settled: NullFace and FLUID edit identity in a frozen diffusion latent space training-free (arXiv 2025), while ID2Face bakes identity/non-identity disentanglement into training to avoid inference-time optimization (Yang et al., arXiv 2025). Two orthogonal directions matter for a deployed platform: CamPro blocks face recognition at the ISP/sensor level, reporting a 99.7% defeat rate against black-box face ID with little impact on person detection (USSLAB, NDSS 2024), and PixelFade protects ReID galleries against recovery attacks via noise-guided progressive replacement (Zhang et al., ACM MM 2024). For private *training* rather than redaction, differential privacy (DP-SGD) with priors and federated/split learning keep raw frames on-device, with DP image classifiers reported around ~86–88% ImageNet top-1 at ε≈8. For real-time edge compliance today, YOLO-based detect-then-blur pipelines and commercial Jetson SDKs remain the only options that are genuinely real-time.

A deep read of DeepPrivacy2 (Hukkelås & Lindseth, WACV 2023) sets realistic expectations for "realistic" anonymization at the edge. It is a detect-then-synthesize GAN that anonymizes people recursively (one individual per forward pass) using three style-based generators — a 256×256 face generator, a CSE dense-pose-guided full-body generator, and an unconditional fallback when CSE detection fails — and reports FID 0.56 on FDF with the lowest pose-estimation endpoint error among anonymization baselines, i.e. it preserves downstream pose/detection signals far better than blur. The deployment reality is the constraint: published throughput is ~7.9 FPS (face) and ~11.6 FPS (full-body) on a *desktop GTX 1080*, **per generator**, and because each person is a separate forward pass, an N-person frame costs ~N passes. On CPU/low-power edge it falls well under 1 FPS, it anonymizes humans only (not plates/screens/text), and identity replacement does not guarantee defeat of every re-identification attack.

|---|---|---|---|
| Detect-then-blur (YOLO ROI) | Pixel-level redaction | Yes (native YOLO FPS) | Real-time, but reversible / harms analytics |
| DeepPrivacy2 (WACV 2023) | Style-GAN inpainting | No (~8–12 FPS/gen on GTX 1080, scales with crowd) | FID 0.56; best pose retention |
| ID2Face / NullFace / FLUID (2025) | Diffusion identity substitution | Unverified at webcam rates *(likely offline)* | SOTA identity suppression + attribute preservation |
| CamPro (NDSS 2024) | Sensor/ISP-level anti-FR | Yes (in-sensor) | 99.7% defeat of black-box face ID |
| PixelFade (ACM MM 2024) | ReID gallery protection | n/a (gallery-side) | SOTA recovery-attack resistance |

*Whether diffusion anonymizers can hit webcam frame rates on consumer/edge hardware is an open question; treat them as offline batch-redaction candidates until benchmarked.*

### Vision foundation models / self-supervised

Self-supervised vision foundation models (VFMs) now yield general-purpose features that rival or beat supervised pretraining, usable with a frozen backbone plus a light task head — which is exactly the property a multi-head real-time platform wants, since one forward pass can be amortized across classification, retrieval, segmentation, depth, and tracking. The dominant recipe is self-distillation on huge curated image sets. DINOv2 set the bar: a frozen ViT-g/14 reaches 86.5% ImageNet-1k linear-probe top-1 and 49.0 ADE20k mIoU, with register tokens cleaning up artifact tokens (Oquab, Darcet et al., TMLR/arXiv 2024). DINOv3 scales to ~1.7B curated images and a ~7B-param teacher, adds Gram anchoring to stop dense-feature degradation over long training, and surpasses DINOv2 by roughly +6 ADE20k mIoU and +6.7 video-tracking J&F — reaching SOTA detection/segmentation with frozen weights (Siméoni, Vo et al., arXiv 2025). A parallel masked-image-modeling line reconstructs masked content or CLIP features: EVA-02 reaches 90.0% ImageNet fine-tune top-1 at only 304M params (Fang, Sun et al., 2023), building on the MAE autoencoder (He et al., 2022). Agglomerative models distill CLIP+DINOv2+SAM into one efficient backbone and fix a resolution "mode switch" via multi-resolution training (Heinrich, Ranzinger et al., RADIOv2.5, NVIDIA, arXiv 2024). For temporal tasks, V-JEPA 2 pretrains on 1M+ hours of video, reaching 77.3% on Something-Something-v2 (Assran, Bardes et al., arXiv 2025).

A deep read of DINOv3 (Siméoni, Vo et al., arXiv 2025) is what makes this usable at the edge: the 6.7B teacher is distilled into small students — ViT-S (21M), ViT-S+ (29M), ViT-B (86M) and ConvNeXt-T/S/B/L (29M–198M) — and the /16 patch size (vs DINOv2's /14) yields fewer tokens, helping latency. ViT-S and ConvNeXt-Tiny are webcam-scale frozen feature extractors; as a proxy, DINOv2 ViT-B runs ~10–12 ms/image at 224px in TensorRT FP16. The important caveats are practical, not just technical: DINOv3 ships under a **custom (non-Apache-2.0) license** with gated weights and a "Built with DINOv3" attribution requirement; the 7B teacher exceeds a 16GB Jetson Orin NX envelope and is offline-only; it is an embedding model, so every task needs a head on top; and how much frozen-feature quality survives INT8 quantization (and how well web-imagery-pretrained features transfer to low-light/motion-blur/fisheye webcam domains) remains an open question.

### Cluster insights

- **Deployment and data engineering, not architecture, now gate real-time edge CV.** The same export/quantize/benchmark pipeline that makes YOLO26 fast (`exporter.py` + `benchmark.py`) is what unblocks every other component here — it is the path to shipping a distilled DINOv3 backbone, the gate that decides whether DeepPrivacy2's GAN anonymization is viable live, and the harness that should empirically settle per-device precision choices rather than trusting vendor-reported speedups. The recurring discipline is on-device benchmarking before any default is changed.
- **A self-improving data flywheel ties three modules into one loop.** Active learning (PPAL-style uncertainty + diversity) identifies the rare/low-confidence classes a small edge model misses on *your* scenes; generative augmentation (ODGEN-style box-conditioned diffusion) then manufactures labeled examples for exactly those classes; and both can share a frozen VFM embedding for selection and clustering. Crucially, all of this runs *offline* between training rounds, so it improves the training data without ever burdening the real-time inference path.
- **The hard open problems are cross-model and cross-constraint, not per-model.** Quantization stability is solved for CNN detectors but unverified for the platform's transformer components (OWLv2, pose, scene-understanding ViTs); realistic anonymization, frozen-VFM feature sharing, and multi-stream scheduling all compete for the same edge power/thermal envelope; and licensing (DINOv3) plus auditable privacy/utility trade-offs (combining sensor-level, generative, and DP layers) are deployment constraints that no single benchmark captures. Optimizing the *pipeline* under a shared budget — not each model in isolation — is the standing challenge.

### Real-time object detection

**Paper:** [YOLOv10: Real-Time End-to-End Object Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.14458) — Wang, Chen, Liu et al. (Ao Wang, Hui Chen, Lihao Liu, Kai Chen, Zijia Lin, Jungong Han, Guiguang Ding) _(NeurIPS 2024 2024)_
**Target module:** `src/models/yolo_wrapper.py, scripts/run_webcam.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** YOLOv10 has two pillars. (1) Consistent dual assignments for NMS-free training: the model keeps a one-to-many head (rich supervision, high recall) alongside a one-to-one head, with a consistent matching metric so both heads rank predictions identically. At inference only the one-to-one head runs, producing a single box per object with no NMS, removing post-processing latency and the IoU-threshold hyperparameter. (2) Holistic efficiency-accuracy driven model design: efficiency-oriented changes (lightweight classification head, spatial-channel decoupled downsampling, rank-guided block allocation) cut redundant compute, while accuracy-oriented changes (large-kernel convolutions, partial self-attention/PSA) add capability cheaply. Together these reshape the accuracy-latency Pareto front. Built on the Ultralytics framework, models ship as N/S/M/B/L/X scales.
- **Headline results:** COCO val, 640x640, T4 GPU TensorRT FP16 (official repo table): YOLOv10-N 38.5% AP / 2.3M params / 6.7G FLOPs / 1.84 ms; -S 46.3% / 7.2M / 21.6G / 2.49 ms; -M 51.1% / 15.4M / 59.1G / 4.74 ms; -B 52.5% / 19.1M / 92.0G / 5.74 ms; -L 53.2% / 24.4M / 120.3G / 7.28 ms; -X 54.4% / 29.5M / 160.4G / 10.70 ms. Headline comparisons from the abstract: YOLOv10-S is 1.8x faster than RT-DETR-R18 at similar AP with 2.8x fewer params and FLOPs; YOLOv10-B has 46% less latency and 25% fewer parameters than YOLOv9-C at the same performance; YOLOv10-L/X outperform/match RT-DETR-R101 (54.3% AP, 259G FLOPs) with far fewer FLOPs (YOLOv10-X ~1.3x faster).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit. YOLOv10-N (2.3M params, 6.7G FLOPs) and -S (7.2M, 21.6G) are tiny and run end-to-end with no NMS, so per-frame post-processing is removed entirely. On a modern consumer GPU (RTX-class), N/S easily exceed real-time at 640px (the T4 reference is 1.84/2.49 ms, i.e. ~400-500 FPS with TensorRT; PyTorch FP16 is slower but still well above 30 FPS). On CPU, expect roughly 10-30+ FPS for the N model at 640px depending on cores and ONNX/OpenVINO backend; drop input to 416-480px or use INT8 to hit 30 FPS on weaker edge boxes. For a live webcam stream: load yolov10n.pt via Ultralytics, run streaming inference, and rely on the one-to-one head (no IoU threshold tuning). The NMS-free path is the key edge win because it makes latency near-constant regardless of crowd density.
- **Integration idea:** In src/models/yolo_wrapper.py, add YOLOv10 as a selectable backbone: the constructor already does YOLO(model_name), so passing "yolov10n.pt"/"yolov10s.pt" works through Ultralytics with no architectural change. Add an end_to_end flag that, when a yolov10* model is detected, sets the predict call to bypass NMS (pass the model's end-to-end/no-NMS path) and skips applying self._iou_threshold, since the one-to-one head emits one box per object. Surface a --model yolov10n.pt option (and document the NMS-free mode) in scripts/run_webcam.py and scripts/run_stream.py, and add yolov10n/s/m to the export + latency sweep in scripts/benchmark.py and scripts/export_model.py so its ONNX/TensorRT numbers are compared head-to-head against the current yolov8n/yolo11n defaults. This gives a near-constant-latency, post-processing-free option on edge hardware with a one-line model swap.
- **Limitations:** The reported speeds are measured on an NVIDIA T4 with TensorRT FP16, not raw PyTorch or CPU, so they overstate throughput on consumer hardware. The NMS-free one-to-one head can incur a small AP drop versus an NMS one-to-many head at equal compute (partly offset by consistent dual-assignment training). PSA/large-kernel modules add some architectural complexity. Pretrained weights are COCO-80-class only (no open-vocabulary). The original THU-MIG repo and Ultralytics distribution are AGPL-3.0, constraining closed-source commercial use.

### Multi-object tracking

**Paper:** [Observation-Centric SORT: Rethinking SORT for Robust Multi-Object Tracking](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.14360) — Cao, Pang, Weng, Khirodkar, Kitani (CMU) _(CVPR 2023 2023)_
**Target module:** `src/tracking/tracker.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** OC-SORT keeps the SORT pipeline (detect, Kalman-predict, IoU-match) but makes association observation-centric via three training-free additions. ORU (Observation-centric Re-Update): when a lost track is re-associated, it builds a virtual trajectory by interpolating between the last and current real observations and re-runs the Kalman filter back along that path, cleaning the noise accumulated during the occlusion gap. OCM (Observation-Centric Momentum): adds a velocity-direction-consistency term to the cost matrix, computing motion direction from actual observations over a window rather than the noisy filter velocity, so matching respects real motion. OCR (Observation-Centric Recovery): a second association stage that matches still-unmatched tracks to detections using the last observed position, recovering objects after short disappearances. All three are detector-agnostic, add negligible compute, and need no appearance/ReID network.
- **Headline results:** Official repo tables (private detections): MOT17-test HOTA 63.2 / MOTA 78.0 / IDF1 77.5; MOT20-test HOTA 62.4 / MOTA 75.9 / IDF1 76.4; DanceTrack-test HOTA 55.1 / MOTA 89.4 / IDF1 54.2 (AssA 38.0); KITTI-cars HOTA 76.5 / MOTA 90.3. The paper abstract reports a stronger DanceTrack HOTA of 62.1. Speed: OC-SORT association runs at ~700 FPS on a single i9 3.0GHz CPU when fed off-the-shelf detections (association cost only; end-to-end speed is dominated by the detector). The tracker is appearance-free, so it adds essentially zero extra model weights/memory on top of the detector.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Excellent fit for consumer webcams and edge devices. OC-SORT is a lightweight, appearance-free (no ReID CNN) post-processor: its association runs at hundreds of FPS even on a CPU, so on a live webcam stream the only real cost is the YOLO detector. With a small detector (e.g. yolo*n/yolo*s) on a consumer GPU or even a modern CPU/edge accelerator, the pipeline stays comfortably real-time (30+ FPS) and tracking adds <1-2 ms per frame. Because it needs no embedding model, it has a tiny memory/compute footprint versus Deep OC-SORT, StrongSORT, or SAM2MOT (the latter is explicitly not yet real-time). It is the natural drop-in to make this repo's tracking robust to occlusion and non-linear motion without spending FPS.
- **Integration idea:** In src/tracking/tracker.py the EnhancedTracker currently wraps Ultralytics BoT-SORT/ByteTrack and bolts on a hand-rolled color-histogram re-ID. Switch the underlying tracker to OC-SORT, which recent Ultralytics ships natively: pass tracker="ocsort.yaml" (or "deepocsort.yaml") to model.track(...) wherever the detector is driven, and expose a tracker_type config option ('botsort'|'bytetrack'|'ocsort'|'deepocsort'). For an Ultralytics-independent, detector-agnostic path, add a thin backend using BoxMOT: `from boxmot import OCSORT; tracker = OCSORT(); tracks = tracker.update(dets, frame)` where dets is Nx6 (x1,y1,x2,y2,conf,cls). OC-SORT's built-in ORU virtual-trajectory recovery largely supersedes the brittle color-histogram match_reid()/lost-track logic in this file, so that custom re-ID code can be retired or kept only as an optional overlay; keep TrackHistory for trajectory visualization and stats.
- **Limitations:** Reported numbers reuse other methods' detections and shared hyper-parameters; the authors note per-dataset tuning can do better, so out-of-the-box numbers are conservative. Pure motion association with no appearance cue means it can still swap IDs under long occlusions or among visually similar, densely crowded targets (lower AssA on DanceTrack vs appearance-based Deep OC-SORT). OCM's velocity-direction cue assumes a roughly stable frame; strong camera motion is not compensated (unlike BoT-SORT's GMC). The 700 FPS figure is association-only, not full detection+tracking.

### Person re-identification

**Paper:** [Omni-Scale Feature Learning for Person Re-Identification](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.00953) — Zhou, Yang, Cavallaro, Xiang _(ICCV 2019 2019)_
**Target module:** `src/tracking/tracker.py (re-ID features)`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** OSNet (Omni-Scale Network) is a lightweight CNN built from a residual block that contains multiple parallel convolutional streams, each with a different receptive-field size (achieved by stacking Lite 3x3 layers) so each stream detects features at one scale. A novel unified aggregation gate (AG) -- a small subnetwork producing input-dependent, channel-wise weights shared across streams -- dynamically fuses these multi-scale feature maps into omni-scale features (dynamic combinations spanning small to large scales). To stay tiny and avoid overfitting on modest ReID datasets, every convolution is factorized into a pointwise (1x1) plus depthwise (3x3) "Lite" convolution. Blocks are stacked layer-by-layer; the network is trained from scratch on ReID benchmarks with softmax/cross-entropy (label smoothing). Inference yields a compact appearance embedding compared by cosine/Euclidean distance.
- **Headline results:** OSNet (osnet_x1_0) ~2.2M parameters, ~978M mult-adds -- roughly 1/10th of ResNet-50 (~25M params). Paper-reported single-query, no re-ranking: Market-1501 84.9% mAP / 94.8% rank-1; DukeMTMC-reID 73.5% mAP; MSMT17 52.9% mAP; CUHK03 also evaluated (six datasets total). Scaled-down osnet_x0_25 has only ~0.2M params yet ~77.8% mAP / 92.2% rank-1 on Market-1501. Note: official torchreid model-zoo softmax-only checkpoint reports a slightly lower 82.6% mAP / 94.2% rank-1 on Market-1501 (the deployable weights). Outputs a compact embedding (512-d in torchreid's feature_extractor).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit for consumer webcams/edge. At ~2.2M params (or ~0.2M for x0.25) it is one to two orders smaller than ViT-based TransReID/CLIP-ReID (~86M+), runs on both CPU and GPU, and exports to ONNX/OpenVINO/TFLite. Reported real-world YOLO11+OSNet pipelines hit ~20 FPS on resource-constrained edge devices; on a consumer GPU it is effectively free relative to the YOLO detector. Per-crop input is 256x128, embeddings (e.g. 512-d) are compared by cosine similarity. For a live webcam: crop each tracked person box, batch the crops through OSNet once per frame (or every N frames), and cache embeddings -- this keeps the added latency to a few ms on GPU and keeps the pipeline interactive. The x0.25/x0.5 variants are the lever for CPU-only or Jetson-class hardware.
- **Integration idea:** In src/tracking/tracker.py, EnhancedTracker._extract_embedding currently builds only a 96-d color histogram (cv2.calcHist over 3 channels x 32 bins) -- a weak, illumination-sensitive descriptor. Replace it with an OSNet appearance embedding: add a deep-person-reid (torchreid) FeatureExtractor loading osnet_x1_0 (or osnet_x0_25 for edge), resize each person crop to 256x128, and return the L2-normalized ~512-d vector instead of the histogram. The existing match_reid cosine-similarity logic and _embeddings dict need no structural change (just bump reid_threshold for the new feature space). Gate it to class_name == 'person', batch crops per frame, and fall back to the histogram if torch/torchreid is unavailable. This upgrades the re-entry re-ID and the BoT-SORT/ByteTrack appearance matching the module wraps, and slots naturally into the repo's ONNX/TensorRT export + benchmarking path.
- **Limitations:** Trained per-domain with supervision; cross-domain/generalization is weaker (the IBN/AIN variants, e.g. osnet_ain, were added later specifically to improve domain generalization). It is a feature extractor only -- it provides no detector, tracker, or temporal association, so it must be paired with YOLO detection + a tracker. Accuracy degrades on heavily occluded or partial bodies (occluded ReID is a separate sub-problem the transformer-based TransReID targets) and on very low-resolution crops. Embeddings are appearance-based, so similarly-dressed people (uniforms) confuse it. Standard benchmark numbers assume reasonably clean person crops.

### Multi-camera / cross-camera tracking & fusion

**Paper:** [MCBLT: Multi-Camera Multi-Object 3D Tracking in Long Videos (originally 'BEV-SUSHI: Multi-Target Multi-Camera 3D Detection and Tracking in Bird's-Eye View')](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/ICCV2025W/AICity/papers/Wang_MCBLT_Multi-Camera_Multi-Object_3D_Tracking_in_Long_Videos_ICCVW_2025_paper.pdf) — Wang, Meinhardt, Cetintas, Yang, Pusegaonkar, Missaoui, Biswas, Tang, Leal-Taixe (NVIDIA / TU Munich / Univ. of Washington) _(ICCV 2025 Workshops (AI City Challenge) 2025)_
**Target module:** `src/multicam/manager.py, scripts/run_multicam.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** Two stages. (1) 3D detection in BEV: each synchronized camera image goes through an image backbone (ResNet-101); a spatial encoder lifts and aggregates the multi-view features using camera intrinsics/extrinsics into one unified BEV feature map; a temporal encoder fuses BEV features over a short window; a DETR-style decoder regresses 3D boxes directly in BEV. (2) Tracking-by-detection in 3D: it adapts SUSHI (Cetintas et al., CVPR 2023), a hierarchical graph neural network that builds graphs over detections and learns associations over progressively longer time spans, to run on 3D/BEV detections, the first 3D hierarchical-GNN tracker. A new near-online global merging block replaces SUSHI's heuristic sliding-window stitching, associating tracks across the full sequence (vs SUSHI's ~512-frame limit) to bridge long occlusion gaps and partial camera coverage.
- **Headline results:** AI City'24 MTMC: 81.22 HOTA, state-of-the-art, with +9.28 HOTA, +2.91 DetA, +4.38 AssA, +1.85 LocA over the prior best. WILDTRACK: 95.6 IDF1, 92.6 MOTA, 93.7 MOTP, beating EarlyBird by +3.3 IDF1 and +3.1 MOTA using the same MVDet/ResNet-18 detections. Detector backbone ResNet-101. Speed: about 1.5 FPS end-to-end on a single NVIDIA A100 with 10 cameras, before any optimization. Scales to full-sequence association (up to roughly 4k frames) vs SUSHI's 512.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Not directly runnable on a consumer webcam in real time as-is. The reported ~1.5 FPS is on a datacenter A100 with 10 cameras and ResNet-101 backbones plus a BEV spatio-temporal transformer and a GNN tracker, before optimization; a 2-4 camera consumer-GPU (or CPU) setup would be sub-1 FPS. It also fundamentally needs calibrated, synchronized, overlapping cameras, which a typical webcam rig lacks. The transferable, real-time-friendly idea is the architecture, not the weights: fuse per-camera 2D detections into a shared ground plane (BEV) via homography from a one-time calibration, then associate in that metric space. For interactive FPS, keep the existing YOLO 2D detector per stream, project detection foot-points to BEV with cv2.findHomography / perspectiveTransform, and do greedy or Hungarian association by BEV distance plus appearance, optionally a lightweight GNN only at low rate. That preserves real-time on edge while borrowing MCBLT's BEV-fusion principle.
- **Integration idea:** Upgrade src/multicam/manager.py: the current find_cross_camera_matches only compares class_id and confidence (no geometry), so it cannot establish true cross-camera identity. Add a BEV fusion path. (1) Add per-camera homography config (4+ image<->ground-plane point pairs) loaded in MultiCameraManager / CameraFeed; compute H with cv2.findHomography once at setup. (2) New method project_to_bev(detections) that maps each detection's foot-point (bbox bottom-center) to common ground-plane (x,y) via cv2.perspectiveTransform. (3) Replace find_cross_camera_matches with a fuse_bev step doing Hungarian matching (scipy.optimize.linear_sum_assignment) on BEV Euclidean distance, optionally gated by ReID embeddings already used in src/tracking, to assign one global ID per physical object. (4) Render a top-down BEV map alongside the grid in create_grid, and expose --bev / --calib flags plus a calibration helper in scripts/run_multicam.py. This gives geometry-grounded cross-camera IDs at real-time speed without MCBLT's heavy 3D detector.
- **Limitations:** Requires calibrated cameras (known intrinsics/extrinsics) and time-synchronized, overlapping multi-view coverage; not applicable to a single uncalibrated webcam or non-overlapping cameras. Far from real-time: ~1.5 FPS on an A100 with 10 cameras pre-optimization, so heavy compute (multi-view ResNet-101 backbones + BEV transformer + GNN). Near-online/offline rather than strictly causal online. Long occlusion gaps without full environment coverage remain hard. Evaluated mainly on indoor, largely synthetic AI City scenes plus small WILDTRACK; generalization to outdoor/dynamic rigs less tested. Depends on detection quality (WILDTRACK numbers reuse EarlyBird detections).

### Small / long-range object detection

**Paper:** [Slicing Aided Hyper Inference and Fine-tuning for Small Object Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.06934) — Akyon, Altinuc, Temizel _(IEEE ICIP 2022 (Bordeaux; pp. 966-970) 2022)_
**Target module:** `src/models/yolo_wrapper.py (SAHI tiling)`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** SAHI has two parts. (1) Slicing Aided Hyper Inference, a model-agnostic, training-free wrapper: the image is cut into a grid of overlapping patches (e.g. 512x512 with ~0.2 overlap), each patch is upscaled and passed through the existing detector so small objects appear larger relative to the receptive field; optionally a full-image pass is added to retain large objects. Per-slice boxes are mapped back to original coordinates and merged via NMS/Greedy-NMM to remove duplicates at slice seams. (2) Slicing Aided Fine-tuning: the detector is additionally fine-tuned on sliced patches (augmented dataset) so its weights adapt to the magnified small-object distribution, compounding the inference-time gain. It plugs onto any detector (Detectron2, MMDetection, YOLOv5/Ultralytics) with no architecture change.
- **Headline results:** Inference-only (no retraining) AP gains on VisDrone+xView: +6.8% FCOS, +5.1% VFNet, +5.3% TOOD. Adding slicing-aided fine-tuning gives cumulative gains of +12.7% (FCOS), +13.4% (VFNet), +14.5% (TOOD) AP. Cost scales with slice count: each patch is a separate forward pass, so a fixed grid is roughly Nx slower than a single full-frame pass (community measurements report ~10x end-to-end slowdown for dense grids, e.g. 12-15 slices, vs single-pass).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Model-agnostic and adds zero params (it wraps the existing YOLO weights this repo already loads), so memory is unchanged — only throughput drops. The catch is the per-slice forward passes: a 1080p frame at 512px/0.2-overlap is ~12-15 slices, i.e. ~10x the per-frame cost. On a consumer GPU (e.g. RTX 3060/T4) a YOLOv8n/YOLO11n that runs >100 FPS full-frame typically drops to roughly 5-15 FPS with a dense grid; on CPU it is sub-1 FPS and not interactive. To run live: make it an opt-in mode (off by default), use a small model (yolo*n), use a coarse 2x2 or 2x3 grid with overlap ~0.2 rather than a fine grid, optionally batch the slices in one model call, and/or trigger slicing only on a region-of-interest or every Nth frame. Single-frame still images are fully real-time-irrelevant. Net: interactive on consumer GPU with a coarse grid; not interactive on CPU.
- **Integration idea:** Add a sliced-inference path to src/models/yolo_wrapper.py: add a `sahi: bool = False` (plus `slice_wh=(512,512)`, `overlap=0.2`) flag to YOLODetector.__init__, and in detect() branch to a `_detect_sliced()` that uses SAHI's get_sliced_prediction over the Ultralytics model (`AutoDetectionModel.from_pretrained(model_type='ultralytics', ...)`). Map the returned object-prediction list back into the existing Detection dataclass (bbox/confidence/class_id/class_name) so all downstream consumers — zone counting, line crossing, tracking/ReID, density heatmaps in src/analytics, and the Streamlit dashboard — work unchanged. Add `sahi` to requirements.txt, expose a 'Tiled / small-object mode' toggle plus a frame-stride control in src/dashboard.py, and keep it off by default in src/stream.py to preserve real-time FPS on full-frame mode.
- **Limitations:** Inference cost scales linearly with the number of slices (one forward pass per patch), so dense grids are far slower than single-pass detection. Slicing removes global context, hurting large objects that exceed one slice (mitigated by adding a full-image pass). Magnifying patches can induce small-object hallucinations / false positives on background. Fixed slice size wastes compute on empty regions when objects are sparse. The big fine-tuning gains require retraining on sliced data, not just inference.

### Promptable segmentation

**Paper:** [SAM 2: Segment Anything in Images and Videos](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.00714) — Ravi, Gabeur, Hu et al. (Meta AI / FAIR) _(ICLR 2025 (arXiv) 2024)_
**Target module:** `src/models/ (new SAM2 segmenter)`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** SAM 2 generalizes SAM to a Promptable Visual Segmentation (PVS) task over video via a streaming transformer. A hierarchical MAE-pretrained Hiera image encoder runs once per frame; a prompt encoder (points/boxes/masks) and a SAM-style two-way mask decoder produce the per-frame mask. New parts: a memory encoder compressing each predicted mask+frame into a compact memory, a FIFO memory bank of recent and prompted frames, and a memory-attention module conditioning the current frame on those memories. Frames are processed one at a time conditioned only on past memory, so it streams over live video; prompts on any frame propagate forward/backward. Trained jointly on images and the new SA-V dataset via a model-in-the-loop data engine.
- **Headline results:** ~44 FPS real-time video (A100). Variants (SAM 2.1, A100, torch 2.5.1): hiera_tiny 38.9M/91.2 FPS, small 46M/84.8, base_plus 80.8M/64.1, large 224.4M/39.5. Video J&F: SA-V test 76.5-79.5, MOSE val 71.8-74.6, LVOS v2 77.3-80.6. ~3x fewer interactions than prior video methods. Images: 6x faster AND more accurate than SAM (58.9 vs 58.1 mIoU 1-click; up to ~61.4 avg over 23 datasets; 5-click mIoU 81.7 over 37 datasets).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong on a consumer GPU, weak on CPU. The tiny/small Hiera variants (38.9M/46M params) reach ~85-91 FPS on A100, so a mid-range RTX (3060/4060) should exceed 30 FPS at 1024px for single-object streaming; base_plus is a good accuracy/speed middle. Pure-CPU webcam use is not real-time. For on-device edge, use distilled siblings (EdgeTAM ~16 FPS iPhone 15 Pro Max, ~22x faster than SAM 2; EfficientViT-SAM) or export the encoder to ONNX/TensorRT. Recipe: GPU + sam2.1_t/s, prompt once (click or YOLO box), propagate via streaming memory; downscale input and cap memory length to bound latency.

### Monocular depth estimation

**Paper:** [Depth Anything V2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.09414) — Yang, Kang, Zhao, Xu, Feng, Zhao (HKU + TikTok/ByteDance) _(NeurIPS 2024 2024)_
**Target module:** `new src/models depth estimator + docs/webcam_depth_research.md`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** A teacher-student pipeline built on a DINOv2 ViT encoder with a DPT regression head (relative inverse depth). Three key practices: (1) train the largest teacher (ViT-G) ONLY on synthetic images, which have pixel-perfect, sharp depth (no real-label noise); (2) scale up teacher capacity to absorb the synthetic-to-real domain gap; (3) bridge that gap by having the teacher pseudo-label 62M+ unlabeled real images, then train smaller student models (ViT-S/B/L) purely on these pseudo-labels rather than on noisy real ground truth. This combines synthetic sharpness with real-world generalization. Models are released at four scales (24.8M / 97.5M / 335.3M / 1.3B). Separate metric-depth variants are obtained by fine-tuning the relative-depth backbone on Hypersim (indoor) and Virtual KITTI 2 (outdoor). A new DA-2K benchmark of high-resolution, sparse relative-depth annotations on hard/ambiguous regions is introduced for evaluation.
- **Headline results:** Model sizes: Small 24.8M, Base 97.5M, Large 335.3M, Giant 1.3B params. Training data: 595K synthetic labeled images (BlendedMVS, Hypersim, IRS, TartanAir, vKITTI2) + 62M pseudo-labeled real images (ImageNet, LSUN, BDD100K, OpenImages, SA-1B, etc.). Speed/accuracy: "over 10x faster" and more accurate than SD-based Marigold/GeoWizard. On the DA-2K relative-depth benchmark, ViT-S reaches 95.3% accuracy vs 86.8% for Marigold. Reported inference: small model under ~10 ms/image on A100 (PyTorch), and ~98.6 ms TensorRT for batch-4 480x300 on Jetson Orin NX (~50 ms on Orin AGX). Metric fine-tunes: max depth 20 m indoor (Hypersim), 80 m outdoor (Virtual KITTI 2).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit, and the reason this is the right pick over the metric contenders. The ViT-Small variant is only 24.8M params (Apache-2.0 licensed), the lightest of any candidate. On a consumer/desktop GPU the small model runs comfortably real-time (community TensorRT builds report ~25-72 FPS depending on resolution/precision; sub-10 ms/image on A100). On edge (Jetson Orin NX) TensorRT gives ~98 ms for batch-4 480x300, i.e. roughly real-time per single stream. On a pure laptop CPU the original PyTorch is ~1-3 FPS, but ONNX Runtime + OpenVINO INT8 at 256-384 input pushes the small model to ~6-20 FPS — interactive. For a live webcam: export ViT-S to ONNX (fabio-sim/Depth-Anything-ONNX), letterbox to 384/256, normalize ImageNet stats, run via onnxruntime (OpenVINO/CUDA EP) or TensorRT, upsample the inverse-depth map back to frame size; run depth every Nth frame if pairing with YOLO to protect loop FPS. Update docs/webcam_depth_research.md (already scaffolded) with the V2 benchmark numbers, the ONNX/OpenVINO INT8 recipe, and the metric-calibration plan; log experiments per its Section 8 milestones.

### Scene-graph generation

**Paper:** [REACT: Real-time Efficiency and Accuracy Compromise for Tradeoffs in Scene Graph Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.16116) — Neau, Santos, Bosser, Buche, Sugimoto _(BMVC 2025 2025)_
**Target module:** `src/analytics/scene_understanding.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** REACT is a one-stage, real-time SGG architecture built on the authors' MIT-licensed SGG-Benchmark. Its central design choice is replacing the standard two-stage Faster-RCNN detector with a single-stage YOLOv8 backbone (later YOLO11/YOLO12, plus open-vocabulary YOLO-World), which alone cuts detection latency ~85% (about 2.75x faster) and raises detection accuracy. A lightweight relation head (a PE-NET-style predicate classifier) then predicts relationships over detected entities. Crucially, REACT filters the number of box proposals before relation classification, drastically cutting the quadratic number of candidate object pairs that must be scored. The paper further introduces Dynamic Candidate Selection (DCS), an inference-time method that adaptively prunes candidate pairs to reduce latency by an average 66.5% without hurting accuracy. The result balances detection accuracy, relation accuracy, and speed in one model.
- **Headline results:** REACT achieves the highest inference speed among existing SGG models: 2.7x faster than SOTA while improving object-detection accuracy by 58%. Reported latency is 32.5ms (YOLOv8m configuration), with a real-time configuration reaching ~23ms; Dynamic Candidate Selection (DCS) reduces latency a further 66.5% on average without performance loss. Swapping Faster-RCNN for YOLOv8 gives an 84.99% latency improvement (~2.75x) and +19.4% F1@K. mean Recall is ~mR@K 18.0 (vs 20.7 for the follow-up REACT++). By contrast the prior one-stage transformer RelTR runs at 16.6 FPS with 63.7M params (R@50 27.5, mR@50 10.8). The follow-up REACT++ reaches 35.8M params and 13.4ms (~55 FPS) via ONNX export.

On a consumer GPU it reports ~23-32.5ms per frame (roughly 30-43 FPS), and the follow-up REACT++ reaches 13.4ms (~55 FPS) with ONNX export, so an interactive 20-30+ FPS webcam stream is realistic on a mid-range GPU. The relation head adds only modest overhead on top of YOLO (REACT++ total ~35.8M params). For a live stream you would run YOLO detection per frame, filter proposals (or use DCS), then score relations on the kept pairs; on CPU expect to drop below interactive rates, so ONNX/TensorRT export and a small YOLO size (n/s) plus aggressive candidate pruning would be needed at the edge.
- **Integration idea:** Add a RelationGraphGenerator to src/analytics/scene_understanding.py that upgrades the current heuristic geometric relations (near/above/inside, computed in _find_relations) into a learned scene graph. Reuse the project's existing ultralytics YOLO detections as REACT's detection stage, then attach REACT's lightweight predicate head (load the MIT-licensed maelic/SGG-Benchmark PSG/VG150 ONNX weights via onnxruntime, matching the repo's existing ONNX export path) to classify semantic predicates (holding, riding, sitting-on) over the top-K filtered detection pairs. Populate the existing ObjectRelation.relation field with the predicted predicate (keep geometric relations as a fast fallback when no GPU/weights), feed the resulting triplets into the Streamlit dashboard and the alert system (e.g. trigger on person-holding-knife), and gate it behind a config flag so the CPU heuristic path stays the default.
- **Limitations:** Accuracy still trails the strongest (slower) two-stage methods on absolute mean-Recall; the follow-up REACT++ notes REACT is not competitive with PE-NET/SQUAT on accuracy. Predicate vocabulary is closed-set and fixed to the training dataset (VG150/IndoorVG/PSG label spaces), unless paired with the open-vocab YOLO-World detector. Reported latencies are GPU figures; CPU-only real-time throughput is not the focus. Like all VG-trained SGG, it inherits long-tailed predicate bias and noisy annotations, and SGG quality is heavily bottlenecked by detector proposal quality. The 23ms vs 32.5ms figures reflect different configurations and should be checked against the exact GPU and YOLO size.

### Camera calibration & geometry

**Paper:** [AnyCalib: On-Manifold Learning for Model-Agnostic Single-View Camera Calibration](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.12701) — Tirado-Garin & Civera (University of Zaragoza, I3A) _(ICCV 2025 2025)_
**Target module:** `src/multicam/manager.py + new src/utils calibration`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** AnyCalib reframes calibration as dense per-pixel ray regression rather than directly regressing a fixed parameter vector. A DINOv2 ViT-L/14 encoder feeds a lightweight DPT-style decoder that predicts a per-pixel field of viewing directions (a FoV field, an intermediate shown by ablation to beat regressing rays directly). Each ray is treated as a point on the sphere manifold and learned in local Euclidean tangent coordinates via log/exp maps (on-manifold), giving stable model-free targets. From the predicted dense rays, intrinsics for a chosen camera model are recovered in closed form by least-squares fitting, so one trained network serves pinhole, Brown-Conrady radial, Kannala-Brandt fisheye, UCM/EUCM and division models, and even handles cropped/stretched (edited) images. The lightweight decoder trims about 20 percent compute (~187.8 GMACs total) versus a standard DPT head.
- **Headline results:** On Stanford2D3D, AnyCalib (pinhole) reaches 1.13 deg angular error, 2.55 deg vertical-FoV error and vFoV AUC@1/5/10 = 21.2/46.8/64.6, beating GeoCalib (1.45 deg angle, 3.23 deg vFoV, vFoV AUC@1=17.4) and 3D foundation models (DUSt3R, MoGe). The general variant anycalib_gen stays close at 1.30 deg / 2.95 deg vFoV while also handling distorted/fisheye inputs. Authors report consistently outperforming GeoCalib, WildCamera and DroidCalib across Stanford2D3D, MegaDepth, TartanAir and LaMAR despite training on orders of magnitude less data. Compute ~187.8 GMACs; backbone DINOv2 ViT-L/14 (~300M params). No FPS/latency table is published; calibration is a one-shot per-camera step, not per-frame.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Not a per-frame model and does not need to be. The DINOv2 ViT-L/14 backbone (~187.8 GMACs, ~300M params, ~320px input) runs on the order of tens of ms on a consumer GPU (comparable ViT-L DINOv2 forwards are ~40-50 ms on an RTX-class card; far slower on CPU), so it cannot sustain 30 FPS live. But intrinsics are static for a fixed lens, so the right pattern is one-shot calibration: grab one (or a few averaged) frames at startup, run AnyCalib once on GPU, cache the resulting K and distortion coeffs, then reuse them every frame for cheap cv2.undistort / cv2.initUndistortRectifyMap remapping in the hot loop. PyTorch-only dependency (Python >=3.10, optional xformers) fits the existing torch stack. For CPU-only edge boxes, run the one-shot calibration offline or export the encoder via the repo's src/deployment ONNX/TensorRT path; per-frame undistortion is pure OpenCV and trivially real-time.
- **Integration idea:** Add src/utils/calibration.py wrapping AnyCalib: a CameraCalibrator.calibrate(frame, cam_model='pinhole'|'radial:2'|'kb:4') returning intrinsics K + distortion coeffs, cached to a per-source JSON sidecar. Wire it into src/multicam/manager.py: add optional intrinsics fields to CameraFeed, run a lazy one-shot calibrate() per camera in MultiCameraManager.start() (GPU once), and apply cv2.initUndistortRectifyMap once + cv2.remap in read_all() so all downstream consumers get undistorted frames. Biggest payoff: replace the heuristic class/confidence matching in find_cross_camera_matches() with geometry-aware association by back-projecting detection centers through each camera's calibrated rays onto a common ground plane (homography), turning guesswork fusion into metric cross-camera matching. Calibrated intrinsics also upgrade src/analytics density heatmaps and zone/line counting to real-world units.
- **Limitations:** Heavy DINOv2 ViT-L/14 backbone (~187.8 GMACs, ~300M params) means inference is GPU-class and not real-time on CPU; unsuitable per-frame on a webcam stream. Trained on panorama-derived crops (~42k images), so very unusual real lenses or extreme distortion may still need a verification pass. Closed-form intrinsics fit requires choosing the camera-model family up front (separate checkpoints for pinhole vs distorted vs edited). Scope is intrinsics + per-pixel rays only, not scene/extrinsic calibration; no official quantitative latency benchmark is published. Estimates are per single image, so noisy frames are best averaged.

### Skeleton-based action recognition

**Paper:** [Channel-wise Topology Refinement Graph Convolution for Skeleton-Based Action Recognition](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.12213) — Yuxin Chen et al. (Chen, Zhang, Yuan, Li, Deng, Hu) _(ICCV 2021 2021)_
**Target module:** `src/models/pose_detector.py + src/analytics/temporal.py, scripts/run_action.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** CTR-GCN introduces Channel-wise Topology Refinement Graph Convolution (CTR-GC). Instead of one adjacency for all channels, it learns a SHARED topology as a generic prior plus a small CHANNEL-SPECIFIC refinement. Each block: (1) linearly transforms joint features; (2) computes pairwise joint correlations (a distance/correlation function on the transformed features) to produce channel-specific topology offsets; (3) adds these offsets to a shared learnable adjacency, giving every channel its own refined, sample-dependent (dynamic) topology; (4) aggregates joint features under these per-channel topologies. This adds very few parameters over a shared-topology GConv. CTR-GC blocks stack with temporal convolutions into the network; final reported numbers fuse a 4-stream ensemble (joint, bone, joint-motion, bone-motion).
- **Headline results:** 4-stream ensemble accuracy: NTU RGB+D 60 = 92.4% (X-Sub) / 96.8% (X-View); NTU RGB+D 120 = 88.9% (X-Sub) / 90.6% (X-Set); state-of-the-art on NW-UCLA. Single CTR-GC model is very small: ~1.46M parameters and ~1.97 GFLOPs (vs ST-GC 1.22M/~1.65G), and in the ablation a single joint stream reaches 84.9% on NTU120 X-Sub. A third-party latency comparison reports CTR-GCN ~22.84 ms per inference (single sample), i.e. ~40+ FPS on a modern GPU.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit for real-time webcam/edge. The model is tiny (~1.46M params, ~1.97 GFLOPs per stream) and consumes joint coordinates directly — exactly the COCO-17 keypoints YOLOv8-pose already outputs — so no heavy heatmap-volume construction (unlike PoseConv3D's 3D-CNN). On a consumer GPU a single stream runs comfortably at interactive FPS (third-party timing ~23 ms/clip => ~40+ FPS); related GCNs at this scale hit 140-190 FPS at batch 1 on an RTX 4080. A live webcam pipeline needs: a per-track sliding window of ~64 normalized keypoint frames feeding the GCN every N frames, run as a single joint-stream (skip the 4-stream ensemble) for latency, optionally ONNX/TensorRT-exported. CPU/edge real time is plausible for the single stream at reduced channels but is not benchmarked in the paper; ONNX-INT8 or a lighter "fast baseline" variant would help on pure-CPU webcams.
- **Integration idea:** Replace the brittle rule-based heuristics in src/models/pose_detector.py (the ActionClassifier class, lines ~187-327) with a learned CTR-GCN head. Concretely: (1) add src/models/ctrgcn.py wrapping a single-stream CTR-GC network (port the official Uason-Chen/CTR-GCN model or use the PYSKL/MMAction2 implementation, exported to ONNX for opencv/onnxruntime inference consistent with src/deployment/exporter.py). (2) In src/analytics/temporal.py, add a per-track keypoint ring buffer (reuse TemporalBuffer/track_id plumbing) that accumulates ~64 frames of normalized COCO-17 keypoints from PoseResult.keypoints. (3) When a window fills, feed it to the GCN and write the predicted action + confidence back into PoseResult.action/action_confidence, so scripts/run_action.py's existing draw/label/summary loop works unchanged. (4) Extend scripts/run_action.py with a --classifier {rules,ctrgcn} flag and a --window-size arg; keep the rules path as fallback. Map the model's NTU label set down to the repo's action vocabulary (or expose full NTU classes). This upgrades the platform from geometric guesses to a real SOTA temporal action model while staying within its YOLO-pose + ONNX/TensorRT stack.
- **Limitations:** Headline accuracy needs a 4-stream ensemble (joint/bone/motion), multiplying inference cost ~4x; a single stream is faster but a few points lower. Designed for trimmed, pre-segmented clips and a fixed temporal window, not native online/streaming detection of action onset/offset. Trained/evaluated on 3D skeletons (NTU) or curated 2D (NW-UCLA); accuracy depends heavily on upstream pose quality and is sensitive to noisy/occluded 2D keypoints (a weakness PoseConv3D explicitly targets). Multi-person interaction modeling is limited; per-sample dynamic correlation adds compute vs a purely static graph. Latency, while ~23 ms on GPU, is higher than later "fast baseline" GCNs and is not benchmarked on CPU/edge in the paper.

### Video anomaly detection

**Paper:** [Learning Memory-Guided Normality for Anomaly Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.13228) — Park et al. (Park, Noh, Ham) _(CVPR 2020 2020)_
**Target module:** `src/analytics/anomaly_detector.py, scripts/run_anomaly.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** MNAD wraps a U-Net-style encoder-decoder with a learnable memory module holding a small set of prototype items that record diverse normal patterns. Encoder features query the memory via attention; read-out items are concatenated with the query and decoded, so the decoder leans on memorized normal prototypes rather than raw CNN capacity. A memory update scheme refreshes items using normal queries, and two new losses (feature compactness, pulling a query toward its nearest item; feature separateness, pushing apart the two nearest items) make features and prototypes discriminative. Two proxy tasks are supported: future-frame prediction or frame reconstruction. The anomaly score combines prediction/reconstruction error (PSNR) with the distance from a query to its nearest memory item; abnormal frames map poorly to normal prototypes, widening the score gap. No optical flow is used.
- **Headline results:** Frame-level AUC, prediction variant: UCSD Ped2 97.0%, CUHK Avenue 88.5%, ShanghaiTech 70.5%. Reconstruction variant: Ped2 90.2%, Avenue 82.8%, ShanghaiTech 69.8%. The memory module adds roughly +2.7% to +4.0% AUC over the no-memory baseline. Speed: ~67 fps (real-time), faster than optical-flow-based competitors. Trained on a single Nvidia GTX TITAN Xp (~1 h / 15 h / 36 h for Ped2 / Avenue / ShanghaiTech). Built on a compact CNN encoder-decoder plus a small memory bank (a fraction of typical autoencoder size); exact parameter/memory footprint not stated in sources fetched.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit for this platform. The reported ~67 fps was on a 2017-class GPU (GTX TITAN Xp); a small CNN encoder-decoder with a tiny memory bank and no optical flow should comfortably exceed 30 fps on a modern consumer GPU, and the team already exports/benchmarks via ONNX/TensorRT so it can be accelerated further. CPU-only real-time is unlikely at full resolution (a few fps at most); on CPU-only edge boxes, run it at low resolution, on grayscale, or every Nth frame. The catch is not inference cost but the per-scene assumption: you must train (or fine-tune) on a short clip of the specific camera's normal footage before live use. Practically: capture ~1-30 min of normal video per webcam, train the prediction model once, ship the .pth plus memory items, and score live frames in a rolling buffer (it needs the preceding frames for future-frame prediction).
- **Integration idea:** Add a second, pixel-level anomaly backend alongside the existing detection-metadata IsolationForest. Create src/analytics/mnad_anomaly.py implementing an MNADAnomalyDetector with the same update()/fit()/check() interface as src/analytics/anomaly_detector.py (so the rest of the app and the Streamlit dashboard are unchanged), wrapping the MNAD prediction encoder-decoder + memory module in torch. Add scripts/train_mnad.py to learn normal patterns from a short clip per source and save weights + memory items, and extend scripts/run_anomaly.py with a --backend {isoforest,mnad} flag plus --mnad-weights. Reuse src/deployment for ONNX/TensorRT export and src/alerts.py to fire on a smoothed PSNR-plus-memory-distance score above a per-camera threshold. This upgrades anomaly detection from "unusual object statistics" to true unsupervised motion/appearance anomalies, while keeping IsolationForest as the zero-training default.
- **Limitations:** Trained per-scene / per-camera on normal-only footage, so it does not transfer to a new camera or layout without retraining or re-fitting the memory; the baseline number assumes a roughly fixed viewpoint (good for a stationary webcam, poor for a moving one). ShanghaiTech AUC (70.5%) is far below Ped2 (97.0%), showing it weakens on multi-scene / crowded data. The anomaly score is uncalibrated PSNR-plus-memory-distance, needing a per-deployment threshold. It detects appearance/motion deviations, not semantic events, and offers no localization beyond per-pixel error maps. Sources fetched did not state exact parameter count, input resolution, or number of memory items.

### Temporal video understanding

**Paper:** [MiniROAD: Minimal RNN Framework for Online Action Detection](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/ICCV2023/html/An_MiniROAD_Minimal_RNN_Framework_for_Online_Action_Detection_ICCV_2023_paper.html) — An, Kang, Han, Yang, Kim (Yonsei University, UC Merced, Google Research) _(ICCV 2023 2023)_
**Target module:** `src/analytics/temporal.py`
**Confidence:** medium

- **Method:** MiniROAD diagnoses the RNN accuracy gap as a train/inference mismatch: training supervises every timestep equally, but at inference only the latest timestep's prediction is used, so early timesteps (with little accumulated context) inject noisy gradients. The fix is a non-uniform loss weighting that down-weights early timesteps and emphasizes later ones, aligning optimization with how the model is actually queried. The model is deliberately minimal: a single-layer GRU (hidden size 1024) that consumes one precomputed clip feature per frame and emits a per-frame class distribution, carrying all history in its hidden state at constant per-frame cost. Inputs are standard two-stream I3D features (RGB + optical flow), Kinetics-pretrained, matching prior OAD work. No transformer, no long-term memory bank, no sliding window.
- **Headline results:** THUMOS'14: 71.8% mAP (Kinetics two-stream I3D features), matching or beating the best transformer OAD methods; TVSeries: 89.6% mcAP; FineAction: 37.1% mAP (numbers from the official jbistanbul/MiniROAD repo). Efficiency: ~10.5M parameters and ~1.08 GFLOPs vs a comparable transformer baseline at ~12.9M params / ~1.96G (roughly half the compute). The GRU itself runs about 200x faster than prior OAD methods (per-frame model latency) on a single RTX 3090; with optical-flow + I3D feature extraction included, end-to-end "Overall FPS" is much lower and is the real bottleneck.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Excellent fit in principle for a streaming webcam loop: the GRU is single-layer (hidden 1024), ~10.5M params / ~1.08 GFLOPs, ingests one feature vector per frame, keeps O(1) state, and needs no future frames or sliding window, so the temporal head is effectively free even on CPU. The catch is the feature extractor: the paper uses two-stream I3D + optical flow, which is too slow for live CPU/consumer-GPU webcam use. To run on a webcam in real time you would drop optical flow and replace I3D with a lightweight RGB clip encoder (e.g. MobileNetV3/X3D-XS or even reusing per-frame CNN features the YOLO stack already computes), feed those into the GRU, and retrain the GRU head with MiniROAD's non-uniform loss. The GRU + lite backbone is plausibly 30+ FPS on a modern consumer GPU and interactive on CPU; expect some accuracy drop from dropping flow.
- **Integration idea:** Add a learned streaming action head to src/analytics/temporal.py. Today that file is purely heuristic/geometric (loitering, abandoned-object, crowd, speed via pixel displacement and durations) with no learned temporal model. Implement a MiniRoadHead class wrapping a single-layer GRU (hidden 1024) that persists its hidden state across frames, mirroring the existing per-frame TemporalBuffer.add() / EventDetector.check() loop. Per frame, extract a lightweight RGB clip embedding (reuse the existing torch backbone or a small X3D/MobileNet), GRU-step it, and emit current-action logits; surface high-confidence transitions as a new EventType.ACTION_RECOGNIZED TemporalEvent so they flow into the existing alert system and Streamlit dashboard. Ship a small trainer in src/training/ that applies MiniROAD's non-uniform per-timestep loss weighting, keeping flow optional/off by default for real-time webcam use.
- **Limitations:** Per-frame model cost is tiny, but the published pipeline still depends on heavy precomputed two-stream I3D features and optical flow, so the real-time bottleneck is upstream feature extraction, not the GRU; the headline FPS isolates the RNN. Accuracy is reported only with Kinetics-pretrained I3D features (not raw RGB end-to-end), so swapping to a lighter RGB-only backbone would change accuracy. It is frame-level action detection (what action is happening now), not temporal localization with precise start/end boundaries. Trained/evaluated on fixed action vocabularies (THUMOS 20 classes, etc.).

### Crowd counting & density estimation

**Paper:** [Rethinking Counting and Localization in Crowds: A Purely Point-Based Framework](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.12746) — Song et al. (Tencent Youtu) _(ICCV (Oral) 2021 2021)_
**Target module:** `src/analytics/heatmap.py, scripts/run_heatmap.py`
**Confidence:** medium

- **Method:** P2PNet (Point-to-Point Network) directly predicts a set of point proposals representing head locations, matching the raw dot annotations used for training. A VGG16-bn backbone with an upsampling/feature-fusion neck produces dense anchor points; two parallel branches regress per-point coordinate offsets and a confidence score. During training, predicted points are matched one-to-one to ground-truth points via the Hungarian algorithm using a cost combining pixel distance and confidence; matched points are positives (pulled toward GT, high confidence) and the rest are pushed to low confidence. At inference, points above a confidence threshold are kept — their count is the crowd size and their coordinates are the localizations. The paper also introduces density-Normalized Average Precision (nAP), which normalizes the match-distance tolerance by local crowd density so evaluation is fair across sparse and dense regions.
- **Headline results:** ShanghaiTech Part A MAE ~52.7 / MSE ~85.1; Part B MAE ~6.25 / MSE ~9.9 (best at publication, ~4.8% MAE and ~12.9% MSE reduction vs prior best ADSCNet). UCF-QNRF MAE ~85.3; also strong on UCF-CC-50 and NWPU-Crowd. Backbone VGG16-bn, ~21.8M parameters. Reported real-time on GPU: ~94.5 FPS (~21.8 ms/frame) in third-party efficiency comparisons, far above VGG-density methods (~23-28 FPS) and HRNet-based STEERER (~17 FPS). Localization measured with the new nAP metric (per-dataset nAP roughly 50-65% depending on threshold).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit. The model is compact (~21.8M params, VGG16-bn) and runs ~94 FPS on a desktop GPU and comfortably 30+ FPS real-time on consumer GPUs (e.g. RTX-class) at typical 720p-1080p webcam frames. On CPU alone expect only a few FPS at full resolution; for a live stream on edge/CPU you would downscale the frame, run inference every Nth frame, and export to ONNX/TensorRT (the repo already has scripts/export_model.py + benchmark.py). Unlike YOLO person detection, P2PNet keeps working in dense scenes (dozens-to-hundreds of heads), so it complements the existing detector rather than replacing it. Practical recipe for the webcam platform: GPU path for live density/count, INT8 TensorRT for edge boxes (Jetson), and frame-skipping + tracker smoothing to stabilize the flickering points.
- **Integration idea:** Add a crowd-counting model that emits point predictions and reuse the existing analytics seam. Create src/models/p2pnet_wrapper.py wrapping the official VGG16-bn P2PNet weights and returning the repo's Detection objects with zero-area boxes centered on each head point plus a 'crowd' class and confidence. Add scripts/run_crowd_counter.py mirroring scripts/run_heatmap.py: stream frames, run P2PNet, overlay the live head count, and feed the point Detections straight into the existing HeatmapGenerator (src/analytics/heatmap.py — it already accumulates detection centers) so dense-crowd heatmaps stop undercounting where YOLO fails. The same point Detections also drop into src/analytics/zone_counter.py for true per-zone occupancy in congested areas, and into the alert system (src/alerts.py) for crowd-density thresholds. Wire an ONNX/TensorRT export path via scripts/export_model.py + scripts/benchmark.py for edge throughput.

Confidence threshold for keeping points must be tuned per scene/density and trades precision vs recall. Performance degrades on very small/blurred far-field heads and under heavy occlusion; the one-to-one Hungarian matching can be sensitive to dense overlapping annotations. Reported FPS is on a server GPU; the paper itself does not provide CPU or edge-device latency, and accuracy is reported on benchmark crops, not arbitrary webcam resolutions/viewpoints.

### Classical OpenCV pipelines

**Paper:** [XFeat: Accelerated Features for Lightweight Image Matching](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.19174) — Potje, Cadar, Araujo, Martins, Nascimento (VeRLab / Google) _(CVPR 2024 2024)_
**Target module:** `src/utils + src/stream.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** XFeat is a compact CNN that, from a single forward pass, predicts three maps at 1/8 resolution: a keypoint heatmap (a parallel branch over 8x8 cells), a dense 64-D descriptor map, and a reliability heatmap. The featherweight backbone keeps spatial resolution high while strictly limiting channels, using aggressive early downsampling and shallow convolutions early, then deeper convolutions (channels roughly tripled as resolution drops) to gain a large receptive field and robustness. Sparse mode matches the compact 64-D descriptors at detected keypoints via mutual nearest neighbors. XFeat* adds an efficient semi-dense mode: it matches coarse 1/8 descriptors, then a lightweight match-refinement module offsets each coarse correspondence toward pixel accuracy without an expensive transformer. It is trained on MegaDepth plus synthetic homographic warps of COCO, yielding less landmark-biased, more generalizable descriptors.
- **Headline results:** Speed (Intel i5-1135G7 laptop CPU, VGA/640x480): sparse XFeat 27.1 FPS, semi-dense XFeat* 19.2 FPS, vs SuperPoint 3.0 FPS and DISK 1.2 FPS — about 9x faster than SuperPoint, and comparable accuracy to DISK at ~16x its speed; up to ~5x faster than competing deep local features overall. GPU: ~1,400 FPS sparse VGA on an RTX 4090, and easily >150 FPS single-batch VGA. Accuracy (relative pose, MegaDepth-1500): AUC@5/10/20 ≈ 42.6 / 56.4 / 67.7, comparable to SuperPoint while much cheaper; it also generalizes to indoor ScanNet-1500 without being trained on it. Compact 64-D descriptors; small enough for embedded deployment.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit for this project's consumer-webcam + edge, no-GPU-required goal. XFeat is the rare learned detector+descriptor that hits real time on a plain laptop CPU: 27.1 FPS sparse / 19.2 FPS semi-dense at VGA on an i5-1135G7 with vanilla PyTorch, and ~1,400 FPS on a desktop GPU, so it keeps up with a 640x480 webcam in real time even CPU-only. Depends only on torch (OpenCV for I/O), Apache-2.0, with community ONNX/TensorRT exports for Jetson/OAK-style edge cameras. To run live: load the pretrained model once, downscale frames to ~VGA, extract features per frame, match to a reference/previous frame with mutual-NN + cv2.findHomography/RANSAC, and budget for CPU NMS/post-processing (thread or batch it). Heavier than ORB but far more robust; on weak edge CPUs, extract every Nth frame and interpolate.
- **Integration idea:** Add src/utils/features.py wrapping XFeat (load via torch.hub 'verlab/accelerated_features'; detectAndCompute -> keypoints + 64-D descriptors; match() doing mutual-NN + cv2.findHomography RANSAC) as an OpenCV-style alternative to ORB/SIFT. Wire it into two existing weak spots: (1) src/multicam/manager.py find_cross_camera_matches — replace the class-id + confidence-difference heuristic with real geometric correspondence (estimate an inter-camera homography/fundamental matrix from XFeat matches to confirm two detections are the same physical object across overlapping views); (2) src/tracking/tracker.py _extract_embedding — augment the color-histogram re-ID with XFeat keypoint matching between a track's stored crop and re-entry candidates for far more robust re-identification. It also enables a new src/utils/stabilize.py computing the homography between consecutive src/stream.py frames for camera-motion compensation, stabilizing density heatmaps and cutting false anomaly/line-crossing triggers on shaky feeds.
- **Limitations:** Accuracy still trails the best heavy semi-dense/dense matchers (LoFTR, ASpanFormer, DISK+LightGlue) at the high-accuracy end; raw mutual-nearest-neighbor matching of the 64-D descriptors needs RANSAC/geometric verification and benefits from a learned matcher (the authors pair it with a "LighterGlue" variant) for best pose results. The headline 27 FPS is measured at VGA with the official PyTorch model on a fairly modern i5; higher resolutions, very low-texture/repetitive scenes, and large illumination extremes still degrade matching, and Python keypoint post-processing/NMS can bottleneck CPU throughput.

### Fall detection & workplace safety

**Paper:** [Pose-Based Fall Detection System: Efficient Monitoring on Standard CPUs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.19501) — Mali & Jaiswal (Vinayak Mali, Saurabh Jaiswal; IIT Kharagpur) _(arXiv 2025)_

**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** MediaPipe Pose landmarks + 6 geometric features + threshold voting over a 20-frame buffer; CPU real-time, sensor-free.
- **Headline results:** On the University of Rzeszow Fall Detection (URFD) dataset and the Multiple Cameras Fall Dataset (MCFD): precision 88.8%, recall 94.1%, F1-score 91.4%, specificity 95.6%. The paper emphasizes CPU real-time operation but does not publish an explicit FPS/latency figure or a measured parameter/memory count.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strongly applicable. The whole pipeline is built to run real-time on a standard CPU with no GPU or sensors: MediaPipe Pose / BlazePose is a small on-device model (single-digit MB) that comfortably hits interactive rates (~20-30 FPS) on consumer laptop CPUs, and the fall logic is just per-frame geometry + a 20-frame ring buffer (negligible cost). On a live webcam stream you would run pose per frame, push 6 features into a deque, and trigger after the voting threshold persists. On the target repo's existing YOLOv8n-pose path (already used in pose_detector.py), this runs even faster on a consumer GPU and is fine on CPU; the only edge concern is single-person assumption and occlusion. This is one of the lightest credible fall detectors for webcam/edge deployment.

Hand-crafted thresholds are less robust than learned classifiers (cf. GCN methods reporting 99%+ accuracy). No explicit FPS, parameter count, or end-to-end latency benchmark is reported, and only two lab/staged datasets are used, so in-the-wild robustness is unproven.

### Edge deployment & model optimization

**Paper:** [YOLO26: Key Architectural Enhancements and Performance Benchmarking for Real-Time Object Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25164) — Khan et al. (Ultralytics-focused survey/analysis) _(arXiv 2025)_
**Target module:** `src/deployment/exporter.py, scripts/export_model.py, scripts/benchmark.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** YOLO26 (released Sep 25, 2025; Ultralytics) reworks the detection head to emit a compact, non-redundant prediction set, giving true end-to-end NMS-free inference, and removes Distribution Focal Loss in favor of a lighter, hardware-friendly direct box parameterization. Removing both NMS and DFL lets exported graphs map cleanly to ONNX, TensorRT, CoreML, and TFLite with fewer custom kernels. Training adds ProgLoss (progressive loss balancing) and STAL (Small-Target-Aware Label assignment) to improve small/occluded-object detection and convergence, plus a new MuSGD optimizer (a hybrid of SGD and the Muon optimizer, inspired by LLM training like Kimi K2) for stable convergence. It is a unified multi-task family (detect, segment, pose, OBB, classify) across five scales (n/s/m/l/x), benchmarked on CPU (ONNX) and NVIDIA T4 (TensorRT FP16) and on Jetson edge devices.
- **Headline results:** Across five detection scales YOLO26 reaches 40.9-57.5 mAP(50-95) on COCO at 1.7-11.8 ms T4 TensorRT(FP16) latency. YOLO26n: ~40.9 mAP, 2.4M params, 5.4B FLOPs, 38.9 ms CPU ONNX, 1.7 ms T4; YOLO26x: 57.5 mAP, 55.7M params, 193.9B FLOPs, ~525.8 ms CPU ONNX, 11.8 ms T4. Mid/large scales: YOLO26m above ~53% and YOLO26l above ~55% mAP at lower latency than transformer detectors. Reports up to 43% faster CPU ONNX inference for YOLO26n vs YOLO11n on an Intel Xeon CPU @ 2.00 GHz. Critically for edge: YOLO26 stays stable under INT8 quantization, whereas RT-DETRv3 shows sharp INT8 accuracy drops.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Strong fit. YOLO26n is 2.4M params / 5.4B FLOPs at ~40.9 mAP — small enough for a live webcam stream. On a consumer GPU it is trivially real-time (1.7 ms on T4 ≈ hundreds of FPS); on CPU, ~38.9 ms ONNX on an Intel Xeon @ 2.00 GHz implies roughly 25 FPS single-thread for the nano model, i.e. interactive 640px webcam inference on a modern laptop CPU via ONNXRuntime, and faster with OpenVINO/INT8 on Intel. NMS-free decoding removes a variable post-process cost (steadier per-frame latency for streaming) and the DFL/NMS removal makes INT8 export robust, unlike RT-DETR. To run on a live webcam: export YOLO26n to ONNX (CPU/OpenVINO) or TensorRT-FP16/INT8 (Jetson/discrete GPU) and feed it the existing OpenCV capture loop; n/s scales for real-time, m/l only with GPU or NPU acceleration.
- **Integration idea:** Adopt YOLO26 as a first-class backbone in the existing export/benchmark pipeline. (1) In src/deployment/exporter.py, change the default model from "yolov8n.pt" to "yolo26n.pt" (or add a model-family arg) — the ModelExporter.export() path already calls ultralytics YOLO(...).export(format=...), so NMS-free/DFL-free YOLO26 exports cleanly to onnx/engine/openvino/coreml/tflite/ncnn with no code change. (2) Add an INT8 quantization path: extend ModelExporter.export() with an int8: bool flag passing int8=True (with a calibration data arg) for openvino/tflite/engine, and have scripts/export_model.py expose --int8 — this directly exploits YOLO26's quantization stability. (3) In scripts/benchmark.py / BenchmarkRunner.compare(), add a head-to-head matrix (YOLO26n FP32 vs FP16 vs INT8 vs YOLO11n/YOLOv8n) on CPU(ONNX) and GPU(TensorRT) so the repo can empirically confirm the "~43% faster CPU" and INT8-stability claims on the target hardware before swapping the default detector used by scripts/run_webcam.py.
- **Limitations:** Most headline numbers are vendor/Ultralytics-reported benchmarks rather than independent third-party reproductions; the paper is largely an architectural overview + benchmarking report, not a from-scratch peer-reviewed method paper, so ablations isolating each component (ProgLoss/STAL/MuSGD) are limited. Exact per-variant CPU ONNX latencies vary with hardware and ONNXRuntime build. NMS-free end-to-end mode can trade a small amount of recall on dense/overlapping scenes versus tuned-NMS pipelines. CPU absolute latencies for larger scales (m/l/x) remain far from real-time without GPU/NPU acceleration.

### Active learning for detection

**Paper:** [Plug and Play Active Learning for Object Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.11612) — Yang, Huang & Crowley (Chenhongyi Yang, Lichao Huang, Elliot J. Crowley) _(CVPR 2024 2024)_
**Target module:** `src/training/active_learner.py, scripts/run_active_learning.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** PPAL is a two-stage, detector-agnostic query strategy that touches neither the model architecture nor the training pipeline (hence "plug and play"). Stage 1, Difficulty Calibrated Uncertainty Sampling (DCUS): it computes per-instance uncertainty from the detector's own outputs, but re-weights it by a category-wise difficulty coefficient that fuses classification difficulty and localization difficulty, so hard/rare classes are not drowned out by easy ones; it then shortlists a candidate pool of the most uncertain images. Stage 2, Category Conditioned Matching Similarity (CCMS): it measures image-to-image similarity as an ensemble of matched instance similarities conditioned on category, then runs k-Means++ over the candidate pool to pick a diverse final query batch. The result combines uncertainty (stage 1) and diversity (stage 2) using only off-the-shelf detector predictions. Implemented on MMDetection.
- **Headline results:** Benchmarked on MS-COCO and Pascal VOC with RetinaNet and Faster R-CNN. In the early active-learning rounds (rounds <=3) PPAL outperforms the previous SOTA MI-AOD/MIAL by 3.4 AP on MS-COCO and 2.8 mAP on Pascal VOC, and outperforms prior work by a large margin overall. Setup: COCO initial labeled set = 2% of images, Pascal VOC initial set = 5%. (Context for the regime: on Pascal VOC, MI-AOD reaches ~72.27% mAP using only 20% of labels, ~93% of the 100%-data performance; PPAL pushes this curve higher.) No FPS/latency/params reported -- it is a data-selection algorithm, not a new detector, so it inherits the speed of whatever detector you plug in.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** PPAL is not a real-time inference model and does not change webcam-time speed -- whatever detector you run stays at its native FPS; PPAL only decides WHICH already-captured frames a human should label between training rounds. That fits this repo's edge story: it makes a small on-device YOLO catch up to a much bigger model on YOUR scenes (your office, your camera angle) using a fraction of the hand-labeled frames, which matters precisely because edge models are small and domain-sensitive. Cost is trivial vs training: stage 1 reuses detector outputs you already produce per frame; stage 2 is an offline k-Means++ over a few hundred candidate images, seconds-to-minutes on CPU, run occasionally -- never on the live stream. Practically: buffer webcam frames + their YOLO predictions, run PPAL nightly/weekly to pick ~50-200 frames to label, fine-tune. No GPU needed for the selection step.
- **Integration idea:** The repo's src/training/active_learner.py currently does pure heuristic uncertainty (UncertaintySampler._compute_uncertainty, lines 98-155: confidence-band + class-conflict + frame-edge scores) with NO diversity step and NO class-difficulty calibration -- so it queues redundant near-duplicate frames. Concretely: (1) Add difficulty calibration to _compute_uncertainty -- track a per-class running difficulty coefficient (e.g. from rolling mean confidence + box-stability/IoU jitter across frames via the existing tracker) and multiply each instance's uncertainty by it, so rare/hard classes survive ranking. (2) Add a new diversity pass, e.g. a select_diverse_batch() method (or a src/training/diversity_sampler.py) that, at export_for_labeling() time, embeds each queued frame (mean of detection crop features, or a lightweight backbone) and runs scikit-learn KMeans++ (sklearn.cluster.kmeans_plusplus -- already a project dep) to pick one representative per cluster -- a practical CCMS stand-in. (3) Surface a scripts/run_active_learning.py flag like --strategy ppal --query-size 100 to run uncertainty-shortlist then diversity-select, replacing the current "top-N by score" behavior, and report the chosen frames to the Streamlit dashboard.
- **Limitations:** No inference-speed/params/memory numbers (it is a selection method, not a detector). Stage 2 (CCMS + k-Means++ over instance-matched similarities) adds an offline clustering cost that grows with candidate-pool size and instances per image -- it runs between training rounds, not per frame, but is heavier than pure uncertainty ranking. Evaluated only on RetinaNet and Faster R-CNN (two-stage / one-stage anchor detectors); not demonstrated on modern YOLOv8/v11 or DETR-style transformers used here. Difficulty coefficients are category-wise, assuming a fixed known label set, which fits poorly with the repo's open-vocabulary (OWLv2) path. A 2026 follow-up (PAL, arXiv:2601.15688) reports PPAL needing ~20.7% more annotations on average than newer reinforced selection, so it is strong-but-not-final SOTA.

### Generative / synthetic data augmentation

**Paper:** [ODGEN: Domain-specific Object Detection Data Generation with Diffusion Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.15199) — Zhu et al. (Jingyuan Zhu, Tsinghua University; co-authors incl. Apple) _(NeurIPS 2024 2024)_
**Target module:** `src/training/augmentation.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** ODGEN is a box-to-image diffusion pipeline. It first domain-adapts a pretrained Stable Diffusion model by fine-tuning its UNet on BOTH cropped foreground objects and full scene images, so it learns the target domain's appearance and per-class look. For layout control it renders synthesized visual prompts (foreground object patches composited at the desired bounding boxes for spatial constraints) paired with object-wise textual descriptions (per-box class text), so each region is conditioned on the right category. Images are generated conditioned on the bbox layout. A trained foreground/background discriminator then audits each generated box: if no synthetic object is detected in a labeled region, that pseudo-label is filtered, cleaning the synthetic annotations. The image+label pairs are mixed with real data to train detectors. Per-object conditioning and domain fine-tuning are what let it generalize to specialized domains where GLIGEN/ControlNet fail.
- **Headline results:** Adding ODGEN synthetic data improves up to +25.3% mAP@.50:.95 on YOLOv5/YOLOv7 across 7 domain-specific Roboflow-100 subsets, the largest gain among compared generative methods (GLIGEN, ControlNet, etc.). On COCO-2014 (general domain) it leads prior methods by up to +5.6% mAP@.50:.95. Protocol: baselines use 200 real images; augmented runs add 5,000 synthetic images (a from-scratch variant trains YOLO on 10k synthetic, validates on 31k real). Built on Stable Diffusion with a fine-tuned UNet. No real-time/FPS numbers are reported (it is an offline data generator, not an inference model); the follow-up FLORA paper notes ODGEN's pipeline assumes enterprise GPUs (e.g., NVIDIA V100) and generation of thousands of samples.
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Not real-time and not an on-device model. ODGEN is a Stable-Diffusion-based offline DATASET FACTORY: each domain needs UNet fine-tuning, then thousands of images are sampled (tens of denoising steps each at ~512px), realistically requiring a V100/A100-class GPU and hours of compute. It cannot run live on a consumer webcam or edge box, and there is no FPS/latency target. The right use for this platform is a one-time/offline data flywheel: generate labeled synthetic images for rare classes the live YOLO model misses (flagged by the existing active-learning module), train/fine-tune a compact YOLO offline, then export to ONNX/TensorRT for the real-time pipeline. The runtime stays the same fast YOLO; only its training data improves. On a consumer GPU (RTX 3090/4090) generation is feasible but slow (minutes for tens of images); CPU-only generation is impractical.
- **Integration idea:** src/training/augmentation.py is misnamed today: its SyntheticGenerator only does classical geometric/photometric/mosaic/cutout transforms. Add a real diffusion backend, e.g. a DiffusionSyntheticGenerator class (or src/training/diffusion_augment.py) wrapping HuggingFace diffusers Stable Diffusion plus a bbox/ControlNet-style spatial condition, implementing ODGEN's two ideas: (1) fine-tune the UNet on cropped foreground crops + full frames captured from the user's own footage, (2) generate images conditioned on synthesized box layouts with per-box class text, then run a foreground/background discriminator filter before writing labels. Reuse the existing export_dataset() YOLO/dataset.yaml writer so output drops straight into the ultralytics training path. Wire the trigger to src/training/active_learner.py: classes/scenes it surfaces as low-confidence become the prompts/boxes ODGEN generates for, closing an offline rare-class data-flywheel that feeds the real-time detector.
- **Limitations:** ODGEN is a heavy OFFLINE pipeline, not a real-time component: it requires per-domain UNet fine-tuning plus generating thousands of synthetic images, needing enterprise-grade GPUs (e.g., NVIDIA V100) per the FLORA follow-up; not feasible on a webcam/edge device. Generation quality and box-faithfulness still degrade for complex scenes with many classes and dense, occluded objects (authors' own stated limitation). Diffusion sampling is slow (tens of denoising steps per image at SD resolution). Gains are reported only for YOLOv5/YOLOv7 and these specific domains; the efficiency follow-up FLORA reports beating the ODGEN baseline (500 vs 5000 synthetic images, up to +21.3% mAP@.50:.95), indicating ODGEN's sample/compute budget is large.

### Privacy-preserving vision

**Paper:** [DeepPrivacy2: Towards Realistic Full-Body Anonymization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.09454) — Hukkelås & Lindseth _(WACV 2023 2023)_
**Target module:** `src/privacy.py, scripts/run_privacy.py`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** A detect-then-synthesize GAN pipeline. People are first detected; each individual is anonymized recursively, one at a time, so crowded scenes are handled sequentially. Three style-based generators are used: a face generator (256x256) for faces, a CSE-guided full-body generator that conditions on dense-pose (Continuous Surface Embeddings) keypoints to synthesize a plausible body in the correct pose, and an unconditional full-body generator as a fallback when CSE detection fails. The CSE conditioning plus surface-guided sampling keeps the generated person aligned to the original pose/region, so downstream pose and detection signals are largely preserved while identity is replaced. The authors also introduce a new large full-body dataset (FDH) to train the body generator, substantially improving image quality and diversity over DeepPrivacy1.
- **Headline results:** FID 0.56 on FDF (128x128), improving on DeepPrivacy1 (FID 0.68). Datasets introduced: FDF256 (242,031 train / 6,533 val images, 256x256, from YFCC100M) and FDH (Flickr Diverse Humans, ~1.5M full-body images). Speed: ~11.6 FPS for the CSE-guided full-body generator and ~7.9 FPS for the face generator on a single NVIDIA GTX 1080 (8GB); a comparison SG-GAN generator ran ~7.3 FPS. Reported to give the lowest pose-estimation endpoint error (EPE) among anonymization baselines and better body/face pose retention than blurring (though noted as unsuitable for fine hand-pose work).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Not real-time on edge as-is. The published 7.9-11.6 FPS is on a desktop GTX 1080 and is per-generator throughput — and it scales DOWN with crowd size because each person is synthesized one at a time, so a frame with N people costs roughly N forward passes. On a CPU-only or low-power edge device it would fall to well under 1 FPS. Realistic deployment on a live webcam stream means: (1) running detection at full FPS but invoking the GAN only on tracked, deduplicated identities; (2) a CUDA consumer GPU (the 1080 figures suggest an RTX 30/40-series could approach ~25-30 FPS single-person); (3) processing at reduced resolution / cropping to person ROIs; or (4) ONNX/TensorRT export (this repo already has scripts/export_model.py + benchmark.py) to cut latency. For multi-person real time at the edge it is currently impractical; it fits an "anonymize-and-store" or low-FPS compliance path better than a live overlay.
- **Integration idea:** Add a RealisticAnonymizer class to src/privacy.py alongside the existing PrivacyFilter, wrapping DeepPrivacy2 (pip install of the hukkelas/deep_privacy2 toolbox) behind the same apply(frame, detections) interface so it is a drop-in upgrade from reversible blur to GAN replacement. Drive it from a new mode (e.g. mode="synthesize") and feed it the person/face bboxes the YOLO detector already produces. Crucially, gate GAN calls through the existing multi-object tracker + re-ID so each identity is synthesized once and cached per track rather than every frame, recovering most of the FPS. Expose it in scripts/run_privacy.py via a --mode synthesize flag and a --device cuda guard that warns/falls back to blur when no GPU is present, and add a DeepPrivacy2 row to scripts/benchmark.py to measure on-device FPS before enabling it live.
- **Limitations:** Inference is slow for real-time/edge (7.9-11.6 FPS on a 1080) and cost scales with the number of people because anonymization is recursive (one person per forward pass). Quality depends on the detector and on CSE dense-pose detection; when CSE fails it drops to a less faithful unconditional generator. Unsuitable for fine hand-pose / precision tasks. Generated humans can show artifacts in unusual poses, occlusion, or heavy crowding, and identity-replacement does not guarantee defeat of every re-identification attack. GAN models are large and GPU-memory hungry, complicating pure-CPU webcam use. Anonymizes humans only (not text, license plates, screens).

### Vision foundation models / self-supervised

**Paper:** [DINOv3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.10104) — Simeoni, Vo, Oquab, Bojanowski, Baldassarre et al. (Meta AI / FAIR) _(arXiv 2025)_
**Target module:** `src/models/base.py (backbone/embeddings)`
**Confidence:** high

- **Method:** DINOv3 trains a 6.7B-param ViT (40 layers, 4096-d, 16x16 patches, RoPE, register tokens) purely self-supervised on LVD-1689M (~1.689B images curated from a 17B pool via hierarchical k-means on DINOv2 embeddings), keeping DINO/iBOT self-distillation (image-level + masked patch-level, no labels). Its key idea, Gram Anchoring, periodically regularizes the student's patch-feature Gram matrix (pairwise patch-similarity structure) toward an earlier cleaner reference, restoring sharp locally-consistent dense features over long training without hurting global features. It adds high-resolution adaptation and post-hoc text alignment for zero-shot. The frozen 7B teacher is distilled into ViT (S 21M, S+ 29M, B 86M, L 300M, H+ 840M) and ConvNeXt (T/S/B/L, 29M-198M) students.
- **Headline results:** ViT-7B frozen backbone (no fine-tuning): ImageNet-1k linear-probe ~88.4% top-1; ADE20K semantic seg 55.9 mIoU (~+6 over DINOv2); NYUv2 depth RMSE 0.309 (best reported); COCO detection 66.1 mAP (frozen backbone + Plain-DETR, SOTA). Pretrained on LVD-1689M (~1.689B images). Distilled: ViT-S 21M, ViT-S+ 29M, ViT-B 86M, ViT-L 300M, ViT-H+ 840M; ConvNeXt-T/S/B/L 29M-198M. Proxy latency: DINOv2 ViT-B ~10-12 ms/img @224 in TensorRT FP16 (~80-100 FPS).
- **Real-time / webcam feasibility:** Use the distilled small students, not the 7B. ViT-S (21M) and ConvNeXt-Tiny (29M) are webcam-scale frozen feature extractors; patch features already power real-time unsupervised video segmentation on Jetson Orin NX via k-means on patch tokens. On a consumer GPU a ViT-S/16 forward at 224-518px is well within interactive rates (DINOv2 ViT-B ~10-12 ms in TensorRT FP16 as proxy); on CPU it is feasible at low res/FPS but ONNX/TensorRT export (already in this repo) is recommended. The /14->/16 patch change vs DINOv2 yields fewer tokens, helping latency. Practically: load facebook/dinov3-vits16 (or convnext-tiny) via transformers, run frozen, cache embeddings; reserve larger variants for offline/batch.
- **Integration idea:** src/models/base.py today only defines BaseDetector (detect / detect_and_track). Add a sibling FeatureExtractor ABC there (embed(frame)->np.ndarray global vector; embed_dense(frame)->grid of patch features), then implement DINOv3Backbone (e.g. src/models/dinov3_backbone.py) loading facebook/dinov3-vits16 via transformers with a frozen forward. Wire its embeddings into the existing re-identification (more robust tracking), scene anomaly detection (k-NN/Mahalanobis on frozen features), density heatmaps and scene understanding (dense patch features), and the active-learning sampler. Export the small variant through the repo's existing ONNX/TensorRT path for edge.
- **Limitations:** Custom DINOv3 License (not Apache-2.0 unlike DINOv2); HF weights are gated and derivatives require a 'Built with DINOv3' attribution. The 7B teacher is far too large for edge (SAM3 + DINOv3-ViT-7B exceeds the 16GB envelope of Jetson Orin NX). It is an embedding/representation model, not a detector: outputs features, so detection/segmentation/depth need a task head on top. Pretraining data is largely Instagram-derived web imagery (domain/bias considerations).

## Appendix C — Completeness critique

The review covers a genuinely broad and well-chosen domain set (24 areas) and the four cluster narratives are coherent, but the effort has a systemic flaw: it conflates 'domain researched' with 'domain implemented,' and several syntheses describe a state of the art the platform does not actually reach. Ground-truthing against the code shows that roughly a third of the claimed domains have NO implementation anchor at all - monocular depth, promptable segmentation (SAM2), camera calibration, crowd density estimation, VLM scene description, and GroundingDINO are research-only with no module or dependency in requirements.txt. Worse, three domains are actively contradicted by the code: re-ID is a 96-dim color histogram (not a deep embedding), 'skeleton-based action recognition' is hand-tuned keypoint geometry (no GCN/transformer), and 'generative/synthetic augmentation' is classical OpenCV transforms with zero generative models despite the class literally being named SyntheticGenerator. Cross-camera 'fusion' matches only on class_id + confidence and cannot associate identities or geometry, and crowd 'counting' is person-track thresholding that fails in exactly the dense regime the domain exists for. The verification process caught real errors (YOLO26 authorship corrected twice; refuted URFD/MCFD fall numbers), which is good, but the recurrence signals the citation layer is fragile and bleeding-edge claims (YOLO26 DFL removal/NMS-free default) need primary sources. Method coverage WITHIN domains is also uneven - the modern tracker generation (OC-SORT/Deep OC-SORT/BoostTrack), tiled small-object inference (SAHI), distilled SAM variants, and metric-vs-relative depth are largely background rather than actionable. Finally, the platform omits entire categories a webcam-CV product must consider - audio-visual, event/thermal cameras, hand/gaze, face analysis and liveness, 3D reconstruction/SLAM/BEV, trajectory forecasting, tracking-by-language, OOD/calibration, adversarial robustness, and notably ethics/bias/EU-AI-Act compliance (privacy is treated only as pixel blur, which is known to be reversible). Net: strong breadth and structure, but the report should explicitly separate 'implemented and benchmarkable' from 'aspirational literature survey,' downgrade the convergence/over-claimed narratives, and prioritize closing the highest-leverage code gaps (deep re-ID, geometric cross-camera fusion, real on-device benchmarking with real images, and a genuine generative pipeline).

**Gaps within covered domains**

- Real-time detection: report frames RT-DETR/RT-DETRv2/v3 and D-FINE/DEIM (the transformer half of the 'two converging families') as background but gives no head-to-head latency/accuracy table vs YOLOv10/YOLO11/YOLO26 on the platform's actual target (CPU/consumer webcam at 640px); the shipped default is still yolov8n.pt, so the 'NMS-free convergence' narrative is not reflected in the codebase or benchmarked.
- Multi-object tracking: no treatment of the post-ByteSORT/BoT-SORT generation that the platform should adopt - Deep OC-SORT, OC-SORT, Hybrid-SORT, BoostTrack/BoostTrack++, and especially camera-motion-compensated trackers for a handheld phone feed. The Kalman-filter vs learned-motion (e.g., MotionTrack/transformer association) tradeoff is unaddressed.
- Person re-ID: the report's 'appearance cues that reuse detector compute' claim is contradicted by the implementation, which uses a 96-dim BGR color histogram (tracker.py:_extract_embedding) - no OSNet/CLIP-ReID/TransReID/SOLIDER embedding, no occlusion-robust or clothes-changing re-ID, no domain-generalization. This is the single largest research-vs-code gap.
- Cross-camera / fusion: find_cross_camera_matches matches only on class_id + a 0.3 confidence-difference window (manager.py:259-272) - there is NO appearance matching, NO epipolar/homography geometry, NO ground-plane/BEV projection, NO global ID assignment. Modern MTMC methods (graph/hierarchical clustering, ReID-gallery association, the AICITY MTMC line) and the 3D-position-from-2D-views goal in idea.md are entirely unrealized.
- Small/long-range detection: missing the concrete toolbox - SAHI/tiled inference, slicing-aware NMS, P2 high-res heads, super-resolution-before-detect, and the relevant benchmarks (VisDrone, TinyPerson, DOTA, SODA-D). For a webcam platform answering 'how does accuracy degrade with distance' (a stated research question) this is under-developed.
- Promptable segmentation: SAM2 is listed as a domain but there is no segmentation module in src/ and no segment-anything dependency; the accuracy/latency ceiling vs distilled variants (MobileSAM, EfficientSAM, EdgeSAM, FastSAM, SAM2.1) and video object segmentation / memory-bank propagation for streaming are described abstractly without an integration plan against the existing Detection.mask field.
- Monocular depth: no depth module or dependency exists. Missing the metric-vs-relative distinction (Depth Anything V2 relative vs Metric3D v2 / UniDepth / Depth Pro metric), temporal consistency for video (no flicker handling), and how depth would feed the 3D-position and small-object goals.
- Scene-graph generation: implementation is a hand-coded distance/direction heuristic over 9 hard-coded scene signatures (scene_understanding.py) - no learned SGG (relation/predicate prediction), no open-vocabulary SGG, no panoptic scene graph; the report should flag that the 'scene graph' domain is essentially unimplemented relative to the literature.
- VLM scene description: claimed as a covered domain but there is NO LLM/VLM client, API key handling, or caption module anywhere; no discussion of edge-deployable VLMs (SmolVLM2, Qwen2.5-VL-3B, Moondream2, FastVLM/LLaVA-OneVision) vs cloud, latency/cost, or grounding/hallucination control for the accessibility-assistant use case in idea.md.
- Camera calibration & geometry: no calibration code (cv2.calibrateCamera/solvePnP/findHomography) despite being a domain; missing intrinsics/extrinsics estimation, lens-distortion correction for webcams, multi-cam extrinsic calibration, and auto-calibration from vanishing points - all prerequisites for the cross-camera 3D goal.
- Skeleton-based action recognition: the report names this domain but the platform uses pure rule-based keypoint geometry (pose_detector.py ActionClassifier) - no ST-GCN/2s-AGCN/CTR-GCN/PoseConv3D/skeleton-transformer, no NTU-RGB+D/Kinetics-Skeleton benchmarking, and the temporal model (LSTM/transformer) promised in idea.md is absent.
- Video anomaly detection: implementation is IsolationForest over hand-crafted scene descriptors (count/position/grid histogram); missing the modern VAD line - memory-augmented autoencoders, Jigsaw/self-supervised, accuracy on UCSD Ped2/Avenue/ShanghaiTech/UCF-Crime, and open-vocabulary/VLM-based anomaly explanation. Frame-level AUC numbers are not engaged.
- Temporal video understanding: temporal.py is rule-based event logic (loiter/abandoned/crowd thresholds); the actual research domain (video-LLMs, long-context streaming understanding, online action detection, TAD) is largely absent, and there is no 'stateful memory' model as idea.md envisions - just deques of bbox centers.
- Crowd counting & density estimation: there is NO density-map model (no P2PNet/CSRNet/DM-Count/CLIP-EBC/PET); 'crowd' is detected by counting person tracks past a threshold (temporal.py _check_crowd), which fails exactly in the dense regime the domain exists to solve. The detection-vs-density crossover point is not characterized.
- Fall detection: corrected GMDCSA-24 numbers are honored, but the section lacks fall-specific benchmarks beyond one CPU paper (no Le2i, UP-Fall, CAUCAFall, FallAllD), no false-alarm-rate discussion (the key deployment metric for elderly care), and no comparison of pose-based vs wearable-fusion vs RGB-temporal approaches.
- Edge deployment: exporter.py covers Ultralytics export formats, but the report's 'joint optimization' claim is not backed by INT8 PTQ/QAT accuracy-drop data, calibration-dataset handling, structured pruning, knowledge distillation, or measured numbers on a real Jetson/Pi/NCNN target - the benchmark harness uses random-noise inputs (exporter.py:197), which does not measure accuracy at all.
- Active learning: report should distinguish uncertainty sampling (entropy/margin/least-confidence) vs diversity/core-set vs learning-loss vs BADGE; for detection specifically, box-level vs image-level acquisition and class-imbalance-aware acquisition are the hard parts and likely under-covered.
- Generative augmentation: the domain name is contradicted by the code - SyntheticGenerator does only classical OpenCV transforms (flip/rotate/cutout/mosaic/noise), with NO diffusion/SDXL/ControlNet/copy-paste/Cut-Paste-Learn. The central research question ('how much synthetic data can replace real') has no diffusion pipeline to answer it, and layout-to-image / annotation-preserving generation is unaddressed.
- Privacy-preserving vision: privacy.py is Gaussian blur/pixelate/blackout on bounding boxes; missing reversible/encryption-based anonymization, face-swap/synthetic-face anonymization that preserves utility, k-same/differential-privacy, federated learning, and the re-identification-attack robustness of naive blur (known to be reversible).
- Self-supervised / foundation backbones: DINOv2/DINOv3, SAM image encoder, and EVA-02 are likely named, but the platform-relevant question - amortizing ONE frozen backbone across detection/depth/segmentation/re-ID heads to save webcam compute - is the stated unifying theme yet has no architectural proposal or shared-feature-cache design.

**Missing topics (not covered)**

- Event / neuromorphic cameras (DVS) and event-RGB fusion - relevant for high-speed motion and low-light, a natural extension of the low-light domain.
- Audio-visual perception - microphones are standard on webcams/phones; audio event detection (glass break, scream, gunshot), sound source localization, and AV fusion for anomaly/fall detection are entirely absent.
- Hand tracking, gesture recognition, and 3D hand pose (MediaPipe Hands, WiLoR) - core for HCI/accessibility uses the platform targets.
- Gaze estimation, eye tracking, and attention - useful for retail analytics, driver monitoring, accessibility.
- Face analysis beyond blurring: face detection/recognition/verification (ArcFace/AdaFace), facial landmarks, expression/emotion, age/gender, anti-spoofing/liveness, and deepfake detection.
- Tracking/segmentation/detection by natural language: referring expression segmentation/comprehension, GroundingSAM, and language-queried video tracking - a clear evolution of the open-vocab domain.
- 3D reconstruction / NeRF / 3D Gaussian Splatting / SLAM / structure-from-motion / Vio - the path from multi-cam 2D to a true 3D spatial model that idea.md explicitly wants.
- Bird's-eye-view (BEV) perception and ground-plane occupancy mapping - the standard representation for multi-camera fusion and zone analytics.
- Trajectory forecasting / motion prediction (Social-GAN, Trajectron++, social-force) - needed to predict collisions, intrusions, and crowd surges proactively.
- Optical flow and motion segmentation (RAFT, GMFlow, SEA-RAFT) - foundational temporal primitive useful for anomaly, action, and background modeling.
- Re-identification of non-person objects and fine-grained recognition (vehicle re-ID/ANPR/license plates, animal/wildlife re-ID) - directly serves the parking-lot and wildlife use cases listed.
- OCR / scene text recognition and document/sign reading - high value for the accessibility-assistant use case.
- Multimodal RGB-D / thermal / IR / NIR sensing - thermal is the dominant modality for reliable person detection in darkness and for fever/industrial monitoring.
- Adversarial robustness, physical adversarial patches, model security, and backdoor/data-poisoning defenses - a surveillance platform is a security target.
- Out-of-distribution / open-set detection and uncertainty calibration - knowing when the model is wrong is critical for safety/anomaly use and is distinct from the IsolationForest scene anomaly.
- Continual / online / test-time adaptation and domain adaptation (sim-to-real, lighting/season drift) - cameras run for months; static models degrade.
- Streaming/online perception latency models (e.g., streaming-perception / sAP metric) and full end-to-end pipeline latency budgeting (decode-infer-track-render), not just single-op benchmarks.
- Video compression / codec-aware CV, ROI encoding, and bandwidth-constrained multi-camera streaming - the systems layer for the 'cheap consumer phones' premise.
- MLOps for vision: data/version management, model registry, drift monitoring, A/B evaluation, and dataset curation/dedup at scale.
- Ethics, bias, fairness, and regulatory compliance (EU AI Act high-risk biometric rules, surveillance governance) - a glaring omission for a person-tracking/biometric-adjacent platform that only treats privacy as pixel blur.
- Synthetic-world / simulation engines for data and digital twins (Unreal/CARLA/Isaac/Kubric, NVIDIA Omniverse) - a more powerful 'synthetic data' route than image-space augmentation.
- Multi-object tracking and counting under heavy occlusion via group/crowd-aware tracking - the bridge between the tracking and crowd-counting domains, owned by neither.

**Weakest / thinnest findings**

- The claim that the literature has 'converged on NMS-free / near-constant-latency inference, appearance cues that reuse detector compute, and TensorRT/ONNX as a first-class target' is asserted as a unifying frame but the platform's own code refutes the appearance-reuse part (color-histogram re-ID) and still defaults to yolov8n.pt - the convergence narrative is over-stated relative to evidence.
- YOLO26 is cited as making NMS-free the default and removing DFL 'for cleaner export'; this product/model is bleeding-edge and the authorship was already corrected twice (Sundaram -> Khan -> Sapkota in the notes), signaling the underlying citation is shaky and the technical claims (DFL removal, NMS-free default) deserve primary-source verification rather than restatement.
- The fall-detection figures required two rounds of correction (the URFD/MCFD numbers were refuted; only GMDCSA-24 at 86.86% acc survives) - a single small-dataset result is thin evidence for any general claim about CPU-based pose fall detection, and 86.86% accuracy with no false-positive-rate context is near-meaningless for deployment.
- Any per-paper accuracy/latency/FPS numbers in the synthesized sections are suspect because the platform's own BenchmarkRunner feeds random-noise images (exporter.py:197) - so internally-generated numbers measure throughput only, never accuracy, and external numbers are not reproduced on the target hardware.
- The 'distilled/quantized variant makes the foundation model survivable on a consumer webcam' arc (segmentation, depth) is presented as established, but with no module, dependency, or measured on-device FPS in the repo it is an untested assertion - especially for SAM2/Depth Anything at webcam resolution and frame rate.
- Cross-camera fusion claims (the B-cluster's 'what is where in the scene') rest on find_cross_camera_matches, which cannot actually associate identities across views (class+confidence only) - the section likely overstates multi-camera capability the platform does not have.
- Crowd-counting coverage is weak-by-construction: with no density model, any stated crowd numbers come from person-track counting, which is known to collapse in dense scenes - claims about crowd estimation accuracy are not supportable by the implementation.
- The 'generative / synthetic data augmentation' domain's findings are the weakest in kind: the code labeled 'SyntheticGenerator' contains no generative model, so any conclusion about synthetic-data efficacy is detached from the platform's actual augmentation (classical transforms).
